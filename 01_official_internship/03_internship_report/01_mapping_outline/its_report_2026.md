# CHAPTER 3: SPECIFIC DETAILS OF PROJECT / TRAINING

## 3.1 Introduction

This chapter explains the main work completed during the industrial training period. The tasks were completed step by step, starting from topic understanding and ending with report preparation and prototype planning.

## 3.2 Initial Research on Cyber Fraud

The first task was to understand the assigned topic. I studied the meaning of cyber fraud, online scams, scam exposure, victimization, and revictimization. I also reviewed common scam types such as phishing, smishing, vishing, investment scams, job scams, loan scams, love scams, e-commerce fraud, malicious APK scams, and recovery scams.

This work helped me understand that cyber fraud does not only involve technical attacks. Many scams also depend on trust, pressure, fake authority, urgency, emotions, and weak verification habits. This understanding became useful when preparing the research framework and module ideas.

## 3.3 Wazuh, SOC, SIEM and CTI Study

During the training, I studied Wazuh as an open-source SIEM and XDR-related security monitoring platform. I reviewed how Wazuh collects logs, monitors endpoints, detects vulnerabilities, supports dashboards, and helps with alert investigation.

I also studied basic SOC, SIEM, XDR, and cyber threat intelligence concepts. This helped me understand how organizations monitor systems, review alerts, and respond to security issues. The study was useful for connecting the research topic with real cybersecurity operations.

## 3.4 Research Report Development

After the initial study, I worked on the cyber fraud research report. The work included preparing chapter drafts, improving the problem background, organizing literature review notes, planning methodology, and aligning the report with the assigned topic.

The report work required repeated reading and editing. Some information was useful for background only, while other information was suitable for the main research argument. I learned to check whether each source and paragraph supported the project topic properly.

## 3.5 Seven-Factor Framework and Module Planning

One important part of the training was preparing the seven-factor revictimization framework. The factors studied included overconfidence, social influence, authority impersonation, urgency and fear, emotional grooming, financial pressure, and protective capability.

These factors were used to plan prevention modules. The module direction included awareness, scam cue detection, response decision-making, verification and reporting, and recovery support. This helped turn the research into a more practical prevention idea.

## 3.6 Evidence, References and Proof Handling

I also worked on evidence and reference handling. Sources were checked and grouped into official sources, academic sources, news sources, vendor threat intelligence, and proof screenshots. This helped make the report more organized and easier to review.

Proof screenshots were used only when they came from real source pages. Conceptual figures were used only to explain ideas and were not treated as source proof. This task helped me understand the importance of using evidence correctly in a research report.

## 3.7 ScamShield-MY Prototype Planning

Later in the training, I planned a prototype idea called ScamShield-MY. The purpose of the idea was to support scam awareness, risk scoring, scenario learning, and reporting guidance.

The prototype plan was kept simple and suitable for the project stage. The main idea was not to build a commercial scam detection system, but to prepare a practical direction that could support the research findings and prevention modules.

## 3.8 Logbook and Report Preparation

The final part of the work involved preparing the internship logbook and this report. The logbook entries were arranged based on daily work, objectives, activities, and achievements. The project folders were also organized to separate official internship documents from research working materials.

# CHAPTER 4: PROBLEMS, SOLUTIONS AND LEARNING OUTCOMES

## 4.1 Introduction

During the training, several problems were faced while completing the research and documentation work. Most of the problems were related to scope, sources, evidence, and report organization.

## 4.2 Understanding the Topic Scope

At the beginning, the topic was broad because cyber fraud includes many scam types and technical areas. To manage this, I focused on revictimization and the factors that make users vulnerable again after scam exposure or victimization.

This helped me prepare a clearer research direction and avoid writing only a general cyber fraud awareness report.

## 4.3 Source Verification

Another problem was source quality. Some sources were useful for examples, but not strong enough for main claims. To solve this, I separated sources by type and used official and academic sources for stronger arguments.

This taught me to be more careful when using statistics, news reports, and vendor threat intelligence.

## 4.4 Wazuh Scope

Wazuh was useful for learning about SOC and security monitoring, but the main research topic focused more on cyber fraud behaviour and prevention. Because of that, Wazuh was kept as a technical study area while the main report focused on revictimization and prevention modules.

## 4.5 Proof Screenshot Handling

Some source pages were difficult to capture because of website blocking, cookie panels, or browser limitations. The solution was to record the limitation and use only available valid screenshots. Generated images were not used as proof.

## 4.6 Report Organization

The report went through several revisions because the materials came from different files and stages of work. I solved this by organizing the folders, separating official internship documents from research materials, and preparing a clearer structure for the final report.

## 4.7 Chapter Summary

The problems faced during the training helped me improve my research discipline, evidence handling, technical judgement, and documentation skills.
