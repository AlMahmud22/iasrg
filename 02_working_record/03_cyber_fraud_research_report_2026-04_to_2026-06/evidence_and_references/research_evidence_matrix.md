## Methodological Implications

| Method area | Evidence-backed requirement | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sampling | Oversample prior victims and distinguish first-time victims, repeat victims, attempted-scam targets, and non-victims. | Revictimization cannot be inferred from general adult victimization data. |
| Measurement | Include direct items for repeat victimization history, repeated contact, recovery scam exposure, shame, reporting delay, and tool-use ability. | Current constructs are plausible but too general. |
| Scenarios | Include local scenarios for PDRM/LHDN/MCMC/BNM/NSRC impersonation, government aid phishing, traffic summons, delivery/e-wallet smishing, investment Telegram groups, job scams, love/parcel scams, malicious APK, and recovery scam. | Malaysia specificity depends on localized cues. |
| Outcome | Score risky compliance, safe verification, reporting intention, reporting speed, and recovery-scam resistance separately. | A single "revictimization tendency" score will be too vague. |
| Intervention evaluation | Use pre/post measures plus scenario tasks; add delayed follow-up if feasible. | Immediate self-confidence gains do not prove behavioural resistance. |

## Raw Audit Conclusion

The report direction is research-valid at the concept level, but not yet evidence-complete. The Malaysia evidence supports a behavioural intervention model, especially around overconfidence, social influence, authority impersonation, urgency, financial opportunity, and verification capability. The weakest area is direct revictimization evidence. Therefore, the next chapter rewrite should not claim that the existing literature proves repeat victimization mechanisms in Malaysia. It should frame revictimization as the study's measured gap and ensure the methodology captures prior-victim recovery, repeated targeting, reporting delay, and recovery-scam vulnerability.
