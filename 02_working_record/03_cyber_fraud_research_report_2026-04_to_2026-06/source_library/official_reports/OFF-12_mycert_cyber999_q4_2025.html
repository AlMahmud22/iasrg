Cyber Incident Summary Report: Quarter 4 2025 

TLP: WHITE
 

1.0 Introduction
The Cyber Incident Quarterly Summary Report Q4 2025 provides an overview of computer security incidents handled by the Cyber999 Incident Response Centre of CyberSecurity Malaysia in Q4 2025.

This quarterly Cyber Incident Report also highlights statistics of incidents dealt with by Cyber999 Incident Response Centre in Q4 2025, according to their categories, cyber security trends observed, and security alerts and advisories released in this quarter. It should be noted that the statistics provided in this report reflect only the total number of incidents reported and handled by the Cyber999 Incident Response Centre, excluding elements such as monetary value or aftermaths of the incidents. Computer security incidents dealt with by the Cyber999 Incident Response Centre involved IP addresses and domains from Malaysia.

CyberSecurity Malaysia also works closely with ISPs, CERTs, Special Interest Groups (SIGs), Government and Law Enforcement Agencies (LEAs), both local and international, to remediate and mitigate computer security incidents affecting Malaysia.

2.0 Trends Observed in Q4 2025
In the fourth quarter of 2025, the Cyber999 Incident Response Centre of CyberSecurity Malaysia recorded a total of 1,881 cyber security incidents, representing a 7 percent decrease compared to the 2,020 incidents reported in Q3 2025. Although the overall number of incidents declined slightly during this quarter, cyber threats remain persistent and continue to evolve in terms of tactics, techniques, sophistication and targets. Fraud-related incidents continued to dominate the cyber threat landscape in Malaysia, followed by data breach and intrusion incidents. These trends indicate that cybercriminals are still heavily exploiting social engineering techniques and credential compromise to target both individuals and organisations in Malaysia.

Fraud incidents accounted for most incidents reported during Q4 2025, with 1,471 incidents recorded, highlighting the continued prevalence of phishing campaigns, impersonation scams, and fraudulent websites. Attackers frequently exploit trusted brands, government agencies, and popular online platforms to deceive victims into revealing sensitive information or conducting fraudulent transactions. In addition, data breach incidents increased to 171 incidents during the quarter, reflecting ongoing risks associated with unauthorised access to organisational systems and databases containing sensitive information such as personally identifiable information (PII) and account credentials.

Meanwhile, intrusion incidents decreased to 101 incidents compared to 173 incidents in the previous quarter, although compromised accounts and website defacements remain common attack vectors. A monthly breakdown of incidents shows that November recorded the highest number of incidents in Q4 2025, with 693 incidents, followed by December with 618 incidents and October with 570 incidents. While other categories, such as malicious codes, intrusion attempts, vulnerability reports, spam, and denial-of-service incidents, were reported in smaller numbers, they continue to pose potential risks to organisations if security measures are not properly implemented. Overall, the trends observed in Q4 2025 emphasise the importance of strengthening cyber security awareness, maintaining updated systems, and implementing effective security controls to mitigate evolving cyber threats. Tables 1 to 3 below provide details of the incidents and their figures reported in Q3 2025 and Q4 2025.

Table 1: Comparison of Incidents Reported in Q3 2025 and Q4 2025

Table 1 presents a comparison of cyber security incidents reported to the Cyber999 Incident Response Centre between Q3 2025 and Q4 2025. Overall, the total number of incidents decreased from 2,020 in Q3 2025 to 1,881 in Q4 2025, representing a 7% reduction. Most incident categories recorded a decline during this period, including Denial of Service (-80%), Intrusion (-42%), Vulnerabilities Report (-41%), Malicious Codes (-37%), Spam (-19%), Intrusion Attempt (-6%), and Fraud (-3%). Despite the overall decrease, Data Breach incidents increased from 142 to 171 incidents, representing a 20% rise, indicating growing concerns related to unauthorised access and exposure of sensitive information belonging to citizens and organisations. Fraud remained the most prevalent category in both quarters, accounting for many reported incidents, highlighting the continued dominance of scam and phishing-related activities within Malaysia’s cyber threat landscape.

Table 2: Breakdown of Incidents Based on Months in Q4 2025

Table 2 presents the monthly breakdown of cyber security incidents reported to the Cyber999 Incident Response Centre during Q4 2025, covering the months of October, November, and December. A total of 1,881 incidents were reported during the quarter, with November recording the highest number of incidents at 693, followed by December with 618 incidents, and October with 570 incidents. Fraud remained the most dominant category throughout all three months, with 411 incidents reported in October, 545 in November, and 515 in December, indicating that scam and phishing-related activities continue to represent the largest cyber threat to Internet users and organisations in Malaysia.

Other categories recorded comparatively lower numbers but remain relevant to the cyber threat landscape. Intrusion incidents were highest in October with 53 incidents before declining to 26 in November and 22 in December, suggesting a decreasing trend towards the end of the quarter. Data breach incidents were most prominent in November with 71 incidents, compared to 51 in October and 49 in December, highlighting an increased exposure of sensitive information during that period. Meanwhile, incidents involving malicious codes, intrusion attempts, vulnerability reports, spam, and denial-of-service attacks were reported in smaller numbers across the three months. Although these incidents were fewer compared to fraud-related incidents, they still represent potential security risks that require continuous monitoring and mitigation by organisations and Internet users.

Table 3: Breakdown of categories and sub-categories of incidents in Q4 2025

Table 3 presents a detailed breakdown of cyber security incidents reported in Q4 2025 based on categories and sub-categories across October, November, and December. Fraud-related incidents continued to dominate throughout the quarter, particularly phishing attacks, which recorded the highest number of incidents with 354 in October, 446 in November, and 459 in December. Other notable fraud sub-categories include impersonation and spoofing, which peaked in November with 80 incidents, fraudulent websites and bogus emails, which were reported in smaller numbers. The data indicates that cybercriminals continue to rely heavily on phishing and social engineering techniques to deceive victims and obtain sensitive information or financial gain.

Other categories also contributed to the overall incident landscape during the quarter. Intrusion incidents were largely associated with account compromise, with 52 incidents reported in October, decreasing to 23 in November and 18 in December, while web defacement incidents remained relatively low. Intrusion attempts were mainly related to login brute force attacks and vulnerability probing activities, suggesting continuous attempts by attackers to identify weaknesses in systems. In addition, malicious code incidents, including malware infections and botnet command-and-control activities, were reported throughout the quarter, though in smaller numbers. Data breach incidents under the content-related category were also significant, particularly in November, with 71 incidents, indicating increased exposure of sensitive information during that period. Overall, the table highlights that while fraud-related incidents remain the most prevalent, other technical attack activities such as intrusion attempts, malware infections, and data breaches continue to pose ongoing risks to organisations and Internet users in Malaysia.

Based on the statistics above, it is notable that most categories of cyber security incidents decreased in Q4 2025 compared to Q3 2025, including Denial of Service, Intrusion, Intrusion Attempt, Vulnerabilities Report, Malicious Codes, Fraud, and Spam incidents. However, Data Breach incidents increased during this quarter, indicating a growing concern regarding the exposure and compromise of sensitive information. In Q4 2025, the most frequently reported incidents were Fraud, Data Breach, and Intrusion. Fraud accounted for the majority of reported incidents, representing approximately 78 percent of all incidents, followed by Data Breach at about 9 percent and Intrusion at around 5 percent.

Looking at the current trends, Fraud, Intrusion, and Data Breach incidents are expected to remain significant cyber security threats in Malaysia. Although the overall number of incidents slightly decreased during this quarter, the increase in data breach incidents highlights ongoing risks associated with unauthorised access to organisational systems and databases. Organisations and Internet users are therefore strongly encouraged to implement proper security measures, strengthen data protection practices, and regularly update their systems to mitigate the risks of data breaches and other cyber threats.

Meanwhile, for fraud incidents other than phishing URLs, new tactics and techniques in online scams continue to emerge, often combining social engineering techniques with malicious applications or malware to deceive victims. These evolving attack methods are expected to continue growing in Malaysian cyberspace, particularly as cybercriminals increasingly exploit digital platforms, mobile applications, and online services to target individuals and organisations. Continuous awareness and vigilance remain essential in preventing users from becoming victims of such fraudulent activities.

2.1 Top Fraud Incidents Reported in Q4 2025
Fraud continuously prevails within the community, targeting various citizens, end users and organisations, from students to professionals, large to small organisations. It has become a preferred method of criminals as awareness is still lacking among the public, making them an easier target. All the fraud incidents were received from organisations and public users. Fake Promotions and Discounts: Popular brands like Lazada, Shopee, or local retailers are spoofed, luring victims with fraudulent discounts or free vouchers.

Subscription Services: Services like Netflix or Spotify are impersonated, tricking victims into renewing subscriptions or fixing payment issues on fake websites. 

      b.Mobile-Focused Phishing (Smishing and App-Based) 

Smishing (SMS Phishing): Attackers send fraudulent SMS messages mimicking banks, e-wallets, or delivery services (for example, J&T, Pos Malaysia) with malicious links. 

      c.Phishing Calls (Vishing) 

Phone Scams: Attackers impersonate government agencies (such as PDRM, LHDN, MCMC), banks, companies, or even CyberSecurity Malaysia, pressuring victims to disclose sensitive information. Common tactics include threats of legal action, account suspension, or overdue payments. 

Therefore, Internet users and organisations must be vigilant when conducting online transactions or performing e-commerce transactions to avoid becoming victims of online fraud.

2.2 Malware Incidents Reported in Q4 2025
The top malware incidents include malware hosting, ransomware, malicious APKs, backdoors, and trojans. The top reported malware incidents are related to malicious APKs. This type of incident is typically received from Internet banking users and sometimes from local financial institutions.

A malicious APK is an Android Package (APK) file containing malware designed to harm devices, steal data, or perform unauthorised actions. APK files are used to distribute and install applications on Android devices, and malicious versions exploit this format to spread malware. They often mimic popular apps (e.g., social media, games, or utilities) to trick users into downloading. Attackers may distribute these files through phishing emails, social media, fake websites, or third-party app stores.

Table 5: Types of Malicious APKs Reported in Q4 2025

The table presents the types of malicious APKs reported in Q4 2025, showing a relatively low but diverse distribution of mobile-based threats. Most of the listed APKs were reported only once, indicating that these threats are scattered and not dominated by a single variant. The only exception is the Bolt Loan APK, which recorded two incidents, making it the most frequently reported among the listed malicious applications. This suggests that while mobile malware activity exists, it is not concentrated in one dominant strain during this period.

A notable pattern in the table is the prevalence of scam-related and loan-based applications, such as Aircond Scam APK, multiple loan APK variants (e.g., Power Loan, Pop Loan, Envolving Loan), and Telegram Scam APK. These types of applications often exploit users through social engineering tactics, particularly financial deception and fraudulent lending schemes. Additionally, the presence of obscure or less recognizable APK names, including com.zhiyuan.yihai APK and a Chinese-named APK, indicates the use of disguised or repackaged applications to evade detection. Overall, the data highlights the opportunistic and varied nature of malicious APK threats, with a strong emphasis on financial scams.

Table 6: Ransomware Variants Reported in Q4 2025

The table summarises the ransomware variants reported in Q4 2025, indicating a relatively low number of incidents distributed across multiple families. Among the listed variants, Qilin recorded the highest number of incidents with two incidents, while all other variants—including Loki Locker, QuickLock, BitLocker, DireWolf, Enzo, and NasLock—were each reported only once. This suggests that ransomware activity during this period was not dominated by a single widespread strain but rather consisted of sporadic incidents involving different variants.

Additionally, the presence of an unidentified ransomware category (NA) highlights challenges in attribution and detection, where certain incidents could not be linked to a specific known variant. The diversity of ransomware families observed reflects the evolving and fragmented nature of ransomware threats, where attackers may use different tools or customised versions to evade detection. Overall, the data indicates a low-frequency but varied ransomware landscape in Q4 2025, emphasising the importance of maintaining broad detection capabilities rather than focusing on a single dominant variant.

Apart from ransomware, we also handled incidents involving botnets that infected computers in Malaysia. A botnet (short for robot network) is a network of computers or devices infected by malicious programs and controlled by a single attacker called a botmaster or bot herder. These infected devices, called bots or zombies, enable the attacker to control them remotely. Botnets are commonly used in:

  1. Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) Attacks: to overwhelm a target system, server, or network, making it unavailable to legitimate users.
  2. Massive Spam Campaigns: Sending large amounts of phishing or spam emails.
  3. Credential Theft: Logging keystrokes to steal passwords or sensitive information.
  4. Cryptojacking: Using infected devices to mine cryptocurrency without consent.

2.3 Data Breach Incident is Prevalent in Malaysia
Data breach incidents remained a major concern in Q4 2025, with 171 incidents reported during the quarter, reflecting an increase compared to the previous quarter. Many of these incidents involved the exposure of personally identifiable information (PII) such as names, identification numbers, contact information (physical address, email address, telephone numbers) and account credentials. In several incidents, attackers attempted to monetise stolen data through extortion or by selling the information on dark web marketplaces. The increasing frequency of data breach incidents highlights the need for organisations to strengthen data protection measures and continuously monitor their systems for unauthorised access.

We are also observing a trend where perpetrators exfiltrate or steal sensitive data from organisations and hold the data hostage, in some incidents, after ransomware attacks, also known as double extortion. Perpetrators will then threaten the organisation to release or sell the data on the dark web unless the organisation pays ransom within a timeframe set by the perpetrators. In the case of extortion by perpetrators, we always advise organisations to refer the matter to the LEAs for the best advice and next course of action. Other trends we observed in this quarter include resurfacing of previous data breaches. Perpetrators claimed and posted on the dark web that they had just breached data belonging to specific organisations. However, our analysis confirmed these are resurfaces of previous data breaches that happened a few years back and not new breaches.

4.0 Conclusion
In conclusion, Q4 of 2025 saw a slight reduction in reported cybersecurity incidents, with a total of 1,881 incidents, down from 2,020 in the previous quarter, representing a 7 percent decline. Despite this slight decrease, the overall threat landscape remains concerning, as cyber incidents continue to occur at a high volume and demonstrate ongoing evolution in attack methods and targets. Fraud-related activities remain the dominant threat, followed by data breach and intrusion incidents, highlighting the continued reliance of cybercriminals on social engineering and credential compromise techniques. These findings underscore the need for sustained vigilance, enhanced user awareness, and stronger cybersecurity measures to effectively mitigate evolving threats. Organisations and individuals must always be vigilant with continuous readiness and preventive and mitigation steps against potential threats. Perpetrators are very motivated, eager, and determined to use new and sophisticated tactics and techniques to execute cyber-attacks. 

Hence, we strongly recommend that all internet users be constantly aware of today's cybercrime trends and adhere to the best cyber hygiene practices. This also includes secure handling of emails from unknown sources, safe web browsing, purchasing goods online, and using social media applications. Users must keep systems up to date with the latest security patches and updates to prevent their computers from being compromised or infected with malware. Always check the legibility of the applications, portal, merchants, services, and products before conducting any online transaction. 

As the complexity of cyber threats continues to increase, organisations and individuals could be potential targets if they are not equipped with security awareness. Providing security awareness campaigns to citizens and organisations is among the best efforts to improve national cyber security and public trust.
