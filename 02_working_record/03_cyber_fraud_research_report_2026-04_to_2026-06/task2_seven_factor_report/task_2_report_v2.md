# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 Focus of the Report

This report explains seven key factors that can increase cyber fraud revictimization among the public in Malaysia. Cyber fraud revictimization means repeated vulnerability after a person has already encountered or experienced a scam. It may involve repeated financial loss, repeated exposure to scam attempts, repeated risky compliance, delayed reporting, fake recovery-scam targeting, or continued vulnerability caused by shame, confusion, pressure, or lack of reporting capability.

The report is written as a structured concept analysis for the wider research topic, **Study on Identification of Factors and Development of Modules for Methods to Prevent Cyber Fraud Revictimization Problems Among Public in Malaysia**. It does not only list scam types. It explains why people may still comply with scam instructions even when they know scams exist, and why prior victims may remain vulnerable after the first incident.

## 1.2 Background of the Topic

Cyber fraud in Malaysia is not only a technical cybersecurity problem. Many scams succeed because victims are persuaded to click links, install applications, transfer money, trust fake authorities, join suspicious investment groups, keep communication secret, or delay reporting after loss. These actions are behavioural, emotional, social, and practical-response issues.

Malaysia-specific evidence shows that fraud remains a major reported cyber incident category. CyberSecurity Malaysia reported fraud as the dominant Cyber999 incident category in Q4 2024 and Q1 2025, with phishing and impersonation or spoofing among the major reported incident types ([CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025a](https://www.cybersecurity.my/portal-main/advisories-details/e14a3424-f7d4-11ef-9a4c-005056812d51); [CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025b](https://www.cybersecurity.my/portal-main/advisories-details/93c2ce07-468d-11f0-a5d3-0050568c1b65)). These reports support the need to study scam behaviour, not only scam technology.

## 1.3 Purpose of the Analysis

The purpose of this report is to arrange the seven factors into a clear framework that can support the main research report, questionnaire design, scenario design, and intervention module development. The seven factors are:

- Overconfidence.
- Social influence.
- Authority impersonation.
- Urgency and fear.
- Emotional grooming.
- Financial pressure.
- Protective capability.

The report connects these factors to three theory foundations: Theory of Planned Behavior, Protection Motivation Theory, and Cognitive Appraisal Theory. These theories help explain intention, threat appraisal, coping confidence, emotional interpretation, and decision-making under pressure.

## 1.4 Structure of the Report

The report first explains the sources and evidence approach. It then defines the main concepts, explains the seven factors one by one, shows how the factors interact, and describes how the framework can be measured and used in an intervention module.

The final references section is grouped by source type so that readers can quickly identify official and regulatory sources, academic sources, case or news evidence, and technical threat evidence. Live proof screenshot candidates are documented separately in `proofs.md` so that proof images can be captured from legitimate source pages before final DOCX formatting.

# 3. Key Concepts

## 3.1 Cyber Fraud

Cyber fraud refers to online or digitally enabled deception where a person is manipulated into giving money, information, access, or trust to a fraudulent party. In this report, cyber fraud includes phishing, smishing, vishing, investment scams, job-task scams, love or parcel scams, malicious APK scams, recovery scams, fake authority calls, fake payment links, and platform-based scams.

Cyber fraud should not be understood only as hacking. Many cases depend on human decision-making under pressure. A scammer may use official-looking messages, fake social proof, emotional grooming, financial reward, fear, urgency, or confusion about reporting channels.

## 3.2 Revictimization

Revictimization means that a person remains vulnerable after a previous scam encounter or victimization event. It does not only mean losing money twice. It can also include repeated scam exposure, repeated risky actions, repeated contact with scammers, delayed reporting, or being targeted by fake recovery services.

For this research, revictimization can be understood through five measurable categories:

- Prior confirmed financial or personal-data loss.
- Repeat loss or repeat scam exposure after the first incident.
- Repeated risky compliance, such as clicking links, installing APKs, or continuing scam contact.
- Recovery-scam targeting after a first victimization event.
- Delayed or avoided reporting after victimization.

## 3.3 Prior Victims and Repeat Risk

Prior victims may not automatically become safer after the first scam. Some may learn from the experience, but others may feel shame, confusion, panic, financial pressure, or loss of confidence. These conditions can increase repeat risk if the victim does not know how to report, verify, seek support, or avoid fake recovery offers.

This is why the study should measure prior-victim status directly. Respondents should not only be asked whether they are aware of scams. They should also be asked about previous scam exposure, previous victimization, repeated contact, reporting behaviour, and scenario-based responses.

## 3.4 Theory Foundations

Three theory foundations support the seven-factor model:

- **Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB):** explains how attitude, subjective norms, and perceived behavioural control influence intention and action (Ajzen, 1991).
- **Protection Motivation Theory (PMT):** explains how people respond to threats based on perceived severity, vulnerability, response efficacy, self-efficacy, and response cost (Rogers, 1975; Rogers, 1983).
- **Cognitive Appraisal Theory:** explains how people interpret stressful or uncertain situations and choose coping responses (Lazarus and Folkman, 1984; Folkman et al., 1986).

Together, these theories explain why scam prevention must go beyond awareness. A person must also believe that verification matters, feel able to perform the safe action, resist social or authority pressure, pause under urgency, and know what to do after a scam.

## 3.5 Seven-Factor Framework

The seven-factor framework is the main structure used in this report. It groups cyber fraud revictimization risk into seven practical categories that can be measured and addressed through intervention modules. Table 1 summarizes the framework before the detailed factor discussion.

| Factor | Short name | Main risk idea | Prevention focus |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| F1 | Overconfidence | Awareness does not become safe action | Build verification habits |
| F2 | Social influence | Fake social proof creates false trust | Verify even when others appear to approve |
| F3 | Authority impersonation | Official-sounding claims trigger compliance | Verify official claims independently |
| F4 | Urgency and fear | Pressure reduces thinking time | Pause before action |
| F5 | Emotional grooming | Trust, shame, and recovery hope increase vulnerability | Reduce shame and increase support-seeking |
| F6 | Financial pressure | Reward or need overrides caution | Verify opportunities before payment |
| F7 | Protective capability | People do not know what to do or where to report | Train tool-use and reporting routines |

# 4. Seven Factors

## 4.1 Overconfidence

### Meaning

Overconfidence happens when a person believes they can detect scams easily without checking other sources. This is risky because cyber fraud often uses familiar logos, normal-looking messages, polished interfaces, and everyday digital routines. A person may know that scams exist but still click a fake link because it looks familiar.

Figure 2 shows the awareness-behaviour gap. The key message is that knowing about scams does not always produce safe behaviour.

![Figure 2: Example of awareness-behaviour gap in familiar digital messages.](figures/fig2.png)

*Figure 2: Example of awareness-behaviour gap in familiar digital messages.*

### Malaysia Evidence

CyberSecurity Malaysia's Q1 2025 report identified local phishing themes such as government aid scams, fake promotions, traffic summons scams, subscription impersonation, bank and e-wallet smishing, and delivery-related messages ([CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025b](https://www.cybersecurity.my/portal-main/advisories-details/93c2ce07-468d-11f0-a5d3-0050568c1b65)). These scams are dangerous because they appear familiar and routine.

Balakrishnan, Ahhmed and Basheer (2025) found that overconfidence significantly predicted online fraud victimization among Malaysian adults ([Balakrishnan et al., 2025](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0317232)). This supports the argument that awareness alone is not enough.

> **Proof capture note:** Proof screenshots for the CyberSecurity Malaysia Q1 2025 page and the academic source were attempted, but the live pages returned blocked or cookie-obstructed captures in headless mode. The source links remain valid as citations, and the capture status is recorded in `proofs.md`.

### Revictimization Link

Overconfidence can increase repeat risk after a person has already avoided one scam attempt. The person may think, "I already know how scams work", and this can reduce caution in a different scam situation. A prior victim may also become careful only with the scam type they experienced before, while still being vulnerable to a new method.

### Measurement Points

Suggested questionnaire or scenario items:

- I can usually tell whether an online message is genuine without checking another source.
- I do not need to verify small payment requests if the message looks familiar.
- Scam warnings are mostly useful for people who are less digitally experienced than me.
- In a fake message scenario, does the respondent verify independently or rely on confidence?

### Intervention Focus

The intervention should teach that verification is not a sign of low digital skill. It is a normal safety routine. Participants should practise checking suspicious links, payment requests, delivery messages, and e-wallet alerts through official channels.

## 4.2 Social Influence

### Meaning

Social influence becomes risky when a person trusts an online offer because it appears to be supported by other people. Scammers may use group chats, fake testimonials, fake comments, fake profit screenshots, celebrity images, or familiar contacts to create social proof.

Figure 3 illustrates this trust-transfer process. The visual shows how apparent group approval can make a scam look safer than it really is.

![Figure 3: Social proof and trust transfer in online scam environments.](figures/fig3.png)

*Figure 3: Social proof and trust transfer in online scam environments.*

### Malaysia Evidence

Securities Commission Malaysia warns that investment scams commonly use social media platforms, fake testimonials, celebrity or prominent-personality endorsement, WhatsApp groups, Telegram groups, and clone entities to appear legitimate ([Securities Commission Malaysia, n.d.](https://www.sc.com.my/investor-empowerment/scam)). Kaspersky also reported malicious APK activity in Malaysia and Brunei where scam messages could spread through messenger-style contexts and appear to come from trusted contacts ([Kaspersky, 2025](https://www.kaspersky.com/blog/tria-stealer-wedding-scam/52958/)).

Balakrishnan et al. (2025) also found that social influence significantly predicted online fraud victimization among Malaysian adults. This supports the need to treat social pressure and trust transfer as measurable factors. The Securities Commission Malaysia proof screenshot is placed later in Section 4.6 because it shows investment-scam warning signs connected to social proof, urgency, and financial pressure.

### Revictimization Link

Social influence can increase revictimization when prior victims join groups looking for recovery help, investment opportunities, or reassurance. Fake recovery groups or fake investment groups may then use social approval to persuade the victim again.

### Measurement Points

Suggested measurement points:

- Trust in offers recommended by online contacts.
- Trust in investment groups that show testimonials.
- Willingness to open files from familiar contacts without verification.
- Ability to separate protective social support from fake social proof.

### Intervention Focus

The intervention should teach that social proof can be fabricated. Participants should verify offers even when they appear to come from friends, groups, agents, testimonials, or familiar contacts.

## 4.3 Authority Impersonation

### Meaning

Authority impersonation occurs when scammers pretend to represent trusted institutions. In Malaysia, scammers may impersonate police, banks, tax authorities, regulators, courts, delivery services, cyber agencies, or support centres. This works because many people feel pressure to obey official-sounding instructions.

Figure 4 shows the safe response pattern. The person should leave the suspicious communication channel and verify independently through trusted sources.

### Malaysia Evidence

CyberSecurity Malaysia's Q1 2025 report identified vishing cases involving impersonation of police, LHDN, MCMC, banks, companies, and CyberSecurity Malaysia ([CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025b](https://www.cybersecurity.my/portal-main/advisories-details/93c2ce07-468d-11f0-a5d3-0050568c1b65)). Securities Commission Malaysia also reports clone-firm scams where fraudsters misuse legitimate names, fake documents, agents, and false credentials ([Securities Commission Malaysia, 2022](https://www.sc.com.my/resources/media/media-release/sc-discloses-findings-on-clone-firm-scams%20)).

The proof screenshot below shows the Securities Commission Malaysia media release on clone-firm scams. It supports this section because the release describes impersonation of legitimate or licensed entities, social media advertisements, agents, WhatsApp chats, and mule-account deposits.

![Proof 3: Securities Commission Malaysia clone-firm scam media release.](proofs/proof3.png)

*Proof 3: Securities Commission Malaysia evidence on clone-firm scam methods and impersonation.*

Bernama reported a PDRM warning about fake Facebook accounts using NSRC's name and image. PDRM stated that NSRC receives calls through 997 and does not contact individuals by phone or WhatsApp ([Bernama, 2025a](https://www.bernama.com/en/news.php/news.php?id=2463516)).

### Revictimization Link

Authority impersonation is especially important after a first scam. A prior victim may trust a fake investigator, fake lawyer, fake bank officer, or fake recovery officer because they want help. This makes recovery scams a direct revictimization risk.

### Measurement Points

Suggested measurement points:

- Pressure felt when a caller claims to be from an enforcement agency.
- Ability to end a threatening call.
- Ability to verify an official claim independently.
- Willingness to avoid caller-supplied numbers, links, or instructions.

### Intervention Focus

The intervention should teach a simple rule: official claims must be verified independently. Participants should not call numbers, click links, or follow instructions supplied by the suspicious caller or sender.

## 4.4 Urgency and Fear

### Meaning

Urgency and fear reduce thinking time. Scammers may say that an account will be frozen, legal action has started, a tax issue must be settled, an investment slot will close soon, or a transfer must be made immediately. These messages create emotional pressure.

Figure 5 shows the core safety routine. The first action under pressure should be to pause, not to comply.

![Figure 5: Urgency as a warning cue and the pause-before-action routine.](figures/fig5.png)

*Figure 5: Urgency as a warning cue and the pause-before-action routine.*

### Malaysia Evidence

CyberSecurity Malaysia's Q1 2025 report identified threats of legal action, account suspension, overdue payments, and similar vishing or phishing themes ([CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025b](https://www.cybersecurity.my/portal-main/advisories-details/93c2ce07-468d-11f0-a5d3-0050568c1b65)). Securities Commission Malaysia also warns that investment scams may use limited-time opportunities to pressure people into acting quickly ([Securities Commission Malaysia, n.d.](https://www.sc.com.my/investor-empowerment/scam)).

The Ministry of Finance Malaysia reported that NSRC had received many scam-related calls, which supports the need for rapid response and reporting capability ([Ministry of Finance Malaysia, 2024](https://www.mof.gov.my/portal/en/news/press-citations/national-scam-response-centre-logs-over-122-000-fraud-calls-since-2022-mof)).

### Revictimization Link

Urgency can be stronger after a first loss. A victim may panic if told that more money will be lost, or may rush to pay a fake recovery fee because they fear losing the chance to recover funds.

### Measurement Points

Suggested measurement points:

- Difficulty pausing when a message says urgent action is needed.
- Confidence in ending threatening calls.
- Tendency to follow instructions when legal or financial consequences are mentioned.
- Scenario response under time pressure.

### Intervention Focus

The intervention should train short refusal scripts and a simple sequence: pause, verify, refuse unsafe instructions, report, and recover. Urgency itself should be treated as a warning cue.

## 4.5 Emotional Grooming

### Meaning

Emotional grooming happens when scammers build trust, attachment, obligation, secrecy, or emotional dependence before asking for money or action. This can appear in love scams, parcel scams, pig-butchering scams, and recovery scams.

Figure 6 presents the process in a respectful way. It shows that emotional grooming and shame are manipulation outcomes, not proof that the victim is careless.

### Malaysia Evidence

Alavi et al. CyberSecurity Malaysia's Q1 2025 report also listed love and parcel scams as fraud sub-categories ([CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025b](https://www.cybersecurity.my/portal-main/advisories-details/93c2ce07-468d-11f0-a5d3-0050568c1b65)).

Bernama's report on fake NSRC Facebook accounts shows that recovery or support infrastructure can itself be impersonated, which is important for post-victimization risk ([Bernama, 2025a](https://www.bernama.com/en/news.php/news.php?id=2463516)). The proof screenshot below supports this point by showing the live news report and PDRM warning about fake NSRC accounts.

![Proof 6: Bernama report on fake NSRC Facebook accounts.](proofs/proof6.png)

*Proof 6: Bernama/PDRM warning that fake NSRC accounts can be used to deceive victims after a scam.*

### Revictimization Link

After victimization, shame and self-blame can make victims avoid reporting or avoid telling trusted people. This isolation can make fake recovery services more persuasive. A victim may pay a fake recovery agent because the offer gives hope after loss.

### Measurement Points

Suggested measurement points:

- Embarrassment about telling family or authorities.
- Willingness to pay someone who claims they can recover lost money.
- Difficulty refusing financial requests from someone trusted emotionally.
- Use of trusted support after a scam incident.

### Intervention Focus

The intervention must avoid victim-blaming. It should teach shame reduction, trusted-contact routines, reporting guidance, recovery-scam warnings, evidence preservation, and emotional support.

## 4.6 Financial Pressure

### Meaning

Financial pressure makes scams attractive when a person needs money, wants to recover previous losses, fears missing profit, or sees an offer as a solution to a financial problem. This factor appears in investment scams, job-task scams, loan scams, e-commerce bargains, e-wallet payment redirection, QR scams, crypto-related offers, and recovery-fee scams.

After a first loss, a victim may want to recover money quickly. This can make new investment schemes, job offers, loans, or recovery-fee claims more attractive. The victim may continue paying because they believe one more payment will unlock the promised return.

### Measurement Points

Suggested measurement points:

- Willingness to act quickly on profitable-looking offers.
- Tendency to skip verification when a limited-time opportunity appears.
- Attraction to online loan, job, or investment offers under financial pressure.
- Ability to check payment accounts and investment legitimacy before paying.

### Intervention Focus

The intervention should include investment, job, loan, e-commerce, e-wallet, QR payment, crypto-related, and recovery-fee scenarios. Participants should practise checking official sources before paying.

## 4.7 Protective Capability

### Meaning

Protective capability refers to the person's ability to perform safe actions. A person may know scams are dangerous but still fail to act safely if they do not know which channel to use, how to verify a claim, what evidence to keep, or how quickly to report after a scam transfer.

### Measurement Points

Suggested measurement points:

- Ability to identify what to do in the first 24 hours after a scam transfer.
- Ability to check suspicious bank accounts, phone numbers, or company names.
- Ability to verify investment legitimacy.
- Belief that fast reporting can reduce harm.

### Intervention Focus

The intervention should teach tool use, not only tool names. Participants should practise choosing the correct channel for bank transfer scams, suspicious accounts, malicious links, investment claims, fake profiles, and recovery scams.

# 5. Factor Interaction

## 5.1 How the Factors Work Together

The seven factors should not be treated as separate personality traits. They are connected parts of a scam and revictimization process. A single scam may use a familiar message, fake social proof, authority claims, urgency, emotional pressure, financial reward, and low reporting capability at the same time.

For example, a fake investment scam may start in a Telegram group, show fake testimonials, claim to be linked to a legitimate entity, promise high returns, pressure the victim to deposit quickly, and request payment into an unrelated account. If the victim does not know how to check official investment sources or suspicious accounts, protective capability becomes the final weakness.

## 5.2 Scam Process View

Table 2 shows how the factors can appear across the scam process. The table helps connect the psychological factors to practical scam moments.

| Stage | Related factors | What happens | Safer response |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| First contact | F1, F2, F3 | Message, call, advertisement, or contact appears familiar, social, or official | Slow down and identify the source |
| Persuasion | F2, F3, F4, F5, F6 | Scammer creates trust, fear, urgency, emotional pressure, or reward expectation | Reappraise the situation as suspicious |
| Decision point | F1, F4, F6, F7 | Victim decides whether to click, pay, install, share, or continue communicating | Pause, verify independently, and refuse unsafe instructions |
| After incident | F5, F7 | Victim may feel shame, confusion, or uncertainty about reporting | Report quickly, preserve evidence, and seek trusted support |
| Repeat targeting | F3, F5, F6, F7 | Fake recovery agents, fake support accounts, or new offers may appear | Avoid recovery fees and use official channels only |

## 5.3 Revictimization Cycle

Revictimization can occur when the outcome of the first scam creates new vulnerability. Shame may reduce reporting. Financial loss may increase pressure to recover money. Confusion may push the victim toward fake help channels. Repeated contact may make the scam feel normal.

# 6. Measurement Design

## 6.1 Respondent Groups

The study should classify respondents into groups so that revictimization can be measured directly instead of being treated as general scam awareness.

- **Prior victims:** people who have already experienced cyber fraud victimization.
- **Repeat victims:** people who experienced more than one victimization event.
- **Attempted-scam exposed non-victims:** people who encountered scam attempts but did not experience confirmed loss.
- **No-known-exposure respondents:** people who do not recall scam exposure.

This classification allows the study to compare awareness, confidence, scenario performance, reporting behaviour, and recovery vulnerability across different exposure levels.

## 6.2 Questionnaire Areas

The questionnaire should move from general to sensitive topics. This helps respondents understand the study before answering questions about victimization.

| Section | Content | Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A | Demographic profile | Understand respondent background |
| B | Digital platform exposure | Identify online banking, e-wallet, e-commerce, messaging, and social media use |
| C | Cyber fraud exposure and victimization history | Classify prior victims, repeat victims, attempted-scam targets, and no-known-exposure respondents |
| D | Seven-factor items | Measure F1 to F7 |
| E | TPB, PMT, and appraisal items | Measure theory constructs |
| F | Scenario tasks | Test actual response under Malaysia-specific scam cues |
| G | Tool-use capability | Test correct use of NSRC, Semak Mule, Cyber999, bank hotline, PDRM, and SC tools |
| H | Intervention feedback | Measure usefulness and pre/post improvement |

## 6.3 Scenario Tasks

Scenario tasks are important because they test behaviour, not only confidence. Respondents should be shown realistic but fictional situations and asked what they would do.

Suggested scenario categories:

- Fake PDRM, LHDN, BNM, MCMC, SC, NSRC, or bank call.
- Fake bank, e-wallet, delivery, traffic summons, or government-aid smishing link.
- Telegram or social media investment offer.
- Job-task or loan-fee scam.
- Love or parcel scam.
- Malicious APK request.
- Fake recovery agent or fake support account.

## 6.4 Scoring Method

Each scenario should be scored separately. The score should measure the response, not just the respondent's confidence.

| Score | Response category | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2 | Safe response | Respondent refuses unsafe action and verifies or reports through the correct channel | Ends fake authority call and checks official channel |
| 1 | Partially safe response | Respondent delays or asks someone but still remains exposed to the scammer | Asks the caller for proof instead of ending the call |
| 0 | Risky compliance | Respondent follows scammer instructions | Transfers money, clicks link, installs APK, pays recovery fee |

The analysis should compare confidence against safe-response scores. A respondent who reports high confidence but chooses a risky action shows an awareness-behaviour gap.

# 7. Intervention Design

## 7.1 Module Overview

The intervention should be practical. It should not only explain what scams are. It should train participants to pause, verify, refuse unsafe instructions, report quickly, preserve evidence, and seek support after victimization.

The five suggested modules are:

- Cyber fraud and revictimization awareness.
- Scam cue detection and cognitive reappraisal.
- Threat and coping appraisal strengthening.
- Verification, reporting, and protective action routine.
- Recovery, shame reduction, social support, and revictimization resistance.

## 7.2 Module-to-Factor Mapping

The table below shows how each module connects to the seven factors. It should guide module design so that no major revictimization factor is left uncovered.

| Module | Main purpose | Factors covered |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Module 1: Awareness | Explain Malaysian scam types, prior victimization, and repeated vulnerability | F1, F3, F5, F6, F7 |
| Module 2: Cue Detection | Train recognition of authority, urgency, social proof, grooming, reward, and technical cues | F1, F2, F3, F4, F5, F6 |
| Module 3: Appraisal | Balance threat awareness with confidence in protective actions | F4, F6, F7 |
| Module 4: Verification and Reporting | Teach practical use of Malaysian verification and reporting channels | F1, F3, F4, F6, F7 |
| Module 5: Recovery Support | Support prior victims and prevent recovery-scam targeting | F2, F3, F5, F7 |

## 7.3 Evaluation Method

The module should be evaluated using pre-test and post-test comparison. If possible, a delayed follow-up should also be used to check whether safer behaviour continues after time has passed.

Suggested outcomes:

- Scenario safe-response score.
- Protective self-efficacy score.
- Response efficacy score.
- Reporting intention.
- Reporting speed intention.
- Recovery-scam resistance.
- Reduction in risky compliance.

# 8. Conclusion

## 8.1 Main Takeaway

Cyber fraud revictimization among the public in Malaysia is not only a technical problem. It is also a behavioural, emotional, social, financial, and practical-response problem. The seven-factor model helps explain why people may remain vulnerable even after they know scams exist or after they have already experienced a scam.

The strongest message from this analysis is that general awareness is not enough. A prevention module must train practical behaviour: pause, verify, refuse unsafe instructions, report quickly, preserve evidence, and recover safely.

## 8.2 Practical Value

The seven-factor framework can support questionnaire design, scenario assessment, intervention module development, and final evaluation. It also helps connect Malaysia-specific scam evidence with behavioural theory.

For prior victims, the report highlights the need to reduce shame, prevent fake recovery scams, strengthen trusted support, and improve confidence in official reporting and verification channels.
