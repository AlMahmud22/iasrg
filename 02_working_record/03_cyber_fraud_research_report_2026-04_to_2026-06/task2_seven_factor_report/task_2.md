# Task 2: Seven Revictimization Factor Categories for the Malaysia Cyber Scam Audit

Audit cutoff: 20 May 2026  
Purpose: define the factor taxonomy that should guide the Malaysia-centric audit of the current report before any chapter rewriting.

## Audit Position

The current report already argues that cyber fraud revictimization is behavioural, cognitive, and Malaysia-specific. That direction is valid, but the factor model must become more explicit before the chapters are rewritten. The seven categories below upgrade the current Chapter 2 factor list into a revictimization-focused taxonomy that can be measured, supported with Malaysian evidence, and translated into intervention modules.

The categories are not independent personality traits. They are audit categories that connect scam exposure, interpretation, coping ability, and repeat vulnerability. Each category should later be operationalized in the questionnaire, scenario tasks, and module evaluation.

## F1. Overconfidence and Awareness-Behaviour Gap

Definition: A victim or potential victim may know that scams exist but still overestimate their ability to detect deception in real time. This becomes a revictimization risk when prior exposure creates misplaced confidence rather than safer routines.

Malaysia evidence anchor:

- Balakrishnan, Ahhmed, and Basheer (2025) found that Malaysian adults generally showed awareness of digital privacy, but overconfidence still significantly predicted online fraud victimization.
- CyberSecurity Malaysia's Q1 2025 Cyber999 report says fraud remains a dominant reported incident category and states that fraud targets citizens from students to professionals, which undermines any simple "only careless users are victims" assumption.

Theory mapping:

- TPB: attitude toward verification may be weak if the person believes they can identify scams without formal checking.
- PMT: response cost increases when checking feels unnecessary.
- Cognitive Appraisal Theory: the first appraisal of a scam cue may be "safe" or "obvious enough" when it should be reappraised as suspicious.

Malaysia scam cues:

- Fake bank or delivery links that look routine.
- Familiar-looking promotions on Shopee, Lazada, WhatsApp, Telegram, Facebook, or Instagram.
- Smishing messages that mimic e-wallets, banks, government aid, or subscriptions.

Questionnaire indicators:

- "I can usually tell whether an online message is genuine without checking another source."
- "I do not need to verify small payment requests if the message looks familiar."
- "Warnings about scams are mostly for people less digitally experienced than me."

Scenario indicators:

- User clicks a realistic delivery, bank, or government-aid link without independent verification.
- User ignores a warning sign because the amount is small or the interface looks polished.

Intervention relevance:

- The module must separate knowledge from performance.
- Scenario-based testing is essential because self-rated awareness is not enough.
- Training should normalize verification as a responsible habit, not as a sign of ignorance.

Evidence priority:

- High: Malaysian peer-reviewed evidence supports overconfidence as a predictor.
- Gap: direct evidence on overconfidence among repeat victims is still limited, so the study must measure prior victimization status.

## F2. Social Influence, Trust Transfer, and Social Proof

Definition: Scammers borrow credibility from people, groups, brands, testimonials, or social platforms. Revictimization risk increases when a victim trusts a message because it appears to come through familiar social channels or because others appear to endorse it.

Malaysia evidence anchor:

- Balakrishnan et al. (2025) found social influence significantly predicted online fraud victimization among Malaysian adults.
- The Securities Commission Malaysia (SC) identifies fake testimonials, social media marketing, celebrity or prominent-personality endorsement, WhatsApp groups, Telegram groups, and clone entities as investment scam tactics.
- Kaspersky's Tria stealer case shows malicious APKs spreading through messenger/social contexts in Malaysia and Brunei, including messages that can appear to come from contacts after account compromise.

Theory mapping:

- TPB: subjective norms and perceived social approval shape intention.
- PMT: social proof can reduce perceived vulnerability and make protective action feel less necessary.
- Cognitive Appraisal Theory: the cue is appraised as legitimate because it is socially familiar.

Malaysia scam cues:

- Telegram investment groups.
- WhatsApp "agent" chats.
- Fake testimonials showing profit.
- Celebrity-endorsed investment schemes.
- APKs sent by a known contact whose account may already be compromised.

Questionnaire indicators:

- "I am more likely to trust an online offer if many people in a group say it works."
- "I would consider an investment opportunity if it is recommended by someone I know online."
- "If a familiar contact sends an attachment, I am less likely to verify it."

Scenario indicators:

- User follows an investment agent after seeing group testimonials.
- User opens an APK sent by a friend/contact.
- User trusts a social media ad using a celebrity image.

Intervention relevance:

- Module content must include social proof manipulation, fake reviews, group pressure, and compromised-contact risks.
- Verification routines should apply even when the message comes from a familiar person.

Evidence priority:

- High: Malaysian academic and regulatory sources support social influence.
- Gap: current report should add sharper distinction between real social support for safety and scammer-created social proof.

## F3. Authority Impersonation and Institutional Compliance

Definition: Scammers impersonate institutions to trigger obedience, fear, or procedural compliance. Revictimization risk is high when the person does not know how legitimate agencies communicate or when prior victimization makes them especially responsive to official-sounding recovery claims.

Malaysia evidence anchor:

- CyberSecurity Malaysia Q1 2025 identifies phishing calls where attackers impersonate government agencies such as police, LHDN, MCMC, banks, companies, or CyberSecurity Malaysia.
- SC clone-firm evidence shows misuse of legitimate names, logos, credentials, websites, and forged documents.
- Bernama reported PDRM warning that fake NSRC Facebook accounts were detected and that NSRC receives calls through 997 but does not call or WhatsApp individuals.

Theory mapping:

- TPB: perceived institutional norms can make compliance feel expected.
- PMT: perceived severity rises when the caller claims arrest, frozen accounts, tax offences, or legal action.
- Cognitive Appraisal Theory: authority cues shape primary appraisal as "threat from a real agency" rather than "possible scam".

Malaysia scam cues:

- PDRM or court money-laundering calls.
- BNM or bank "safe account" claims.
- LHDN tax threats.
- MCMC line-blocking claims.
- SC guarantee-deposit or public-register impersonation.
- Fake NSRC recovery or help accounts.

Questionnaire indicators:

- "If a caller claims to be from an enforcement agency, I feel pressured to follow instructions quickly."
- "I know how to independently verify whether a call from PDRM, BNM, LHDN, MCMC, SC, or NSRC is genuine."
- "I would hesitate to end a call from someone claiming to be an officer."

Scenario indicators:

- User remains on a fake authority call despite secrecy instructions.
- User transfers money to a "safe account".
- User contacts a fake NSRC social media account.

Intervention relevance:

- Scenarios must include local authority names and the correct official response.
- The module should teach that official verification must use independently sourced contact details, not numbers or links supplied by the caller.

Evidence priority:

- High: official and regulatory sources strongly support authority impersonation as a Malaysian scam pattern.
- Gap: current report must not treat "institutional trust" as only positive; it can also be exploited.

## F4. Urgency, Fear, and High-Arousal Compliance

Definition: Scammers compress time and raise emotional arousal so that victims comply before verifying. Revictimization risk increases when earlier loss creates panic, fear of losing more money, or desperation to resolve the situation quickly.

Malaysia evidence anchor:

- CyberSecurity Malaysia Q1 2025 lists threats of legal action, account suspension, and overdue payments as vishing tactics.
- SC warns that investment scams are promoted as "limited time only" opportunities.
- MOF reported NSRC call volumes and losses, while official reporting guidance emphasizes fast reporting because delayed action reduces the chance of blocking funds.

Theory mapping:

- PMT: high perceived severity without high response efficacy can produce panic or unsafe compliance.
- Cognitive Appraisal Theory: primary appraisal becomes "urgent danger" and secondary appraisal becomes "no time to check".
- TPB: intention to verify collapses if the person perceives time pressure as overriding normal behaviour.

Malaysia scam cues:

- "Your account will be frozen."
- "You are linked to money laundering."
- "Pay now to avoid police action."
- "This investment slot closes today."
- "Call/transfer within minutes."

Questionnaire indicators:

- "When a message says urgent action is needed, I find it difficult to pause."
- "I am confident I can end a threatening call and verify through official channels."
- "Fear of legal or financial consequences makes me more likely to follow instructions."

Scenario indicators:

- User acts before verifying under legal, banking, tax, or account-suspension pressure.
- User chooses immediate payment to avoid a claimed penalty.

Intervention relevance:

- The protective routine must make "pause" the first trained action.
- Scenario feedback should teach that urgency itself is a cue, not just a situational detail.

Evidence priority:

- High for scam cue evidence.
- Medium for direct emotional-arousal measurement; the study must operationalize fear and urgency rather than only mention them.

## F5. Emotional Grooming, Shame, and Post-Victim Recovery Vulnerability

Definition: Emotional grooming creates attachment, obligation, secrecy, and dependency. After victimization, shame and hope for recovery can increase vulnerability to recovery scams, fake investigators, and repeated targeting.

Malaysia evidence anchor:

- Alavi et al. (2018) studied love scam victims in Selangor and identified sweet talk, socializing, easy internet access, self-pressure, fake social media profiles, false promises, parcel narratives, and tax-agency impersonation.
- CyberSecurity Malaysia Q1 2025 records love and parcel scam as a fraud sub-category.
- SC clone-firm evidence shows many victims did not cooperate with investigation, which is relevant to shame, low perceived recovery value, and reporting reluctance.
- PDRM/NSRC warnings about fake NSRC accounts show that recovery infrastructure itself can be impersonated.

Theory mapping:

- Cognitive Appraisal Theory: grooming changes appraisal from "stranger risk" to "trusted relationship".
- TPB: shame and norms affect help-seeking and reporting intention.
- PMT: low self-efficacy and high response cost reduce recovery action after loss.

Malaysia scam cues:

- Online romantic partner requests money.
- Parcel or customs fee attached to a relationship.
- Fake recovery agent promises to retrieve money for a fee.
- Fake NSRC, lawyer, investigator, or regulator contact.
- Secrecy instruction framed as trust or protection.

Questionnaire indicators:

- "I would feel embarrassed to tell family or authorities if I lost money to a scam."
- "After being scammed, I would be tempted to pay someone who claims they can recover my money."
- "I find it difficult to refuse financial requests from someone I trust emotionally."

Scenario indicators:

- User continues contact after repeated small requests.
- User pays a recovery fee after prior scam loss.
- User avoids reporting because of embarrassment.

Intervention relevance:

- Module 5 must not be optional if the study claims to address revictimization.
- Recovery content should include shame reduction, trusted-contact routines, fake recovery warnings, evidence preservation, and reporting steps.

Evidence priority:

- Medium-high: Malaysian love-scam evidence exists, but repeat-victim recovery evidence is thinner.
- Gap: the current methodology must define and recruit prior victims clearly enough to measure post-victim vulnerability.

## F6. Financial Need, Opportunity Pressure, and Reward/FOMO Bias

Definition: Financial pressure, opportunity seeking, and fear of missing out can make risky offers appear worth pursuing. Revictimization risk increases when a prior loss creates a desire to recover money quickly or when small initial losses encourage further payments.

Malaysia evidence anchor:

- SC warns that investment scams promise high returns with little or no risk, use social media platforms, request deposits into personal or unrelated accounts, use limited-time framing, ask for more money through taxes/fees, use fake testimonials, and impersonate legitimate entities.
- SC's clone-firm findings show scams targeting investors expecting large returns quickly and using mule accounts to receive funds.
- SC Annual Report 2025 identifies online scams and unlicensed operators using corporate mule accounts, e-wallets such as Touch 'n Go and Shopee Pay, and crypto-related suspicious URLs.
- Bernama/Fortinet reported that phishing, fake e-commerce platforms, fraudulent payment links, QR codes, and festive-period digital payments contributed to 2025 scam exposure.

Theory mapping:

- TPB: positive attitude toward fast gain can weaken protective intention.
- PMT: perceived reward competes with perceived threat; response cost seems high if verification might make the user miss out.
- Cognitive Appraisal Theory: the situation is appraised as opportunity rather than threat.

Malaysia scam cues:

- "Invest RM200 and get RM10,000 within 24 hours."
- Telegram/WhatsApp investment groups.
- Job task payments.
- Non-existent loans or upfront fees.
- Fake e-commerce discounts.
- E-wallet and QR payment redirection.
- Crypto investment and clone-platform offers.

Questionnaire indicators:

- "I am willing to act quickly if an online offer looks profitable."
- "I may skip verification if an investment opportunity is available for a limited time."
- "Financial pressure makes online loan, job, or investment offers more attractive."

Scenario indicators:

- User deposits money into a personal or unrelated account.
- User continues paying "tax", "unlock", or "task" fees.
- User trusts fake profit screenshots or testimonials.

Intervention relevance:

- Modules must include investment and job-scam scenarios, not only phishing.
- Verification must include SC Investment Checker, SC Investor Alert List, Semak Mule, and licensed-entity checks.

Evidence priority:

- High: Malaysian regulatory evidence is strong.
- Gap: current report should better connect financial pressure and FOMO to revictimization after previous loss.

## F7. Protective Self-Efficacy, Verification Friction, and Recovery/Reporting Capability

Definition: Even when victims perceive risk, they may not act safely if they do not know what to do, doubt the effectiveness of action, or perceive reporting as embarrassing, slow, confusing, or futile.

Malaysia evidence anchor:

- Cyber999 is the national point of contact for reporting and responding to computer security incidents, with online form, email, hotline, and mobile app channels.
- Semak Mule is a PDRM portal used to check suspicious bank accounts, phone numbers, and company names.
- NSRC 997 receives scam reports and public advisory calls; MOF reported 122,603 calls by August 2024 and RM383 million in suspicious transactions blocked in 2023.
- SC advises investors to use SC Investment Checker, deal only with licensed or registered entities, and check Semak Mule before investing.
- BNM AR 2024 release reports that the National Fraud Portal reduced the time taken by financial institutions to identify suspected fraud transactions and increased mule-account identification.

Theory mapping:

- PMT: self-efficacy and response efficacy are central.
- TPB: perceived behavioural control predicts protective intention and behaviour.
- Cognitive Appraisal Theory: secondary appraisal depends on knowing realistic coping options.

Malaysia scam cues:

- User does not know whether to call bank, NSRC 997, PDRM, Cyber999, SC, or MCMC.
- User searches online and finds fake support accounts.
- User reports late because they feel ashamed or confused.
- User cannot tell the difference between Semak Mule, Investment Checker, and Cyber999.

Questionnaire indicators:

- "I know exactly what to do in the first 24 hours after a scam transfer."
- "I can check whether a bank account, phone number, or company name has scam reports."
- "I know where to verify whether an investment entity is licensed."
- "I believe reporting quickly can reduce harm."

Scenario indicators:

- User selects correct report path based on scam type.
- User preserves evidence and contacts the bank/NSRC quickly.
- User avoids fake support or recovery accounts.

Intervention relevance:

- The intervention must teach tool use, not only tool names.
- Pre-test/post-test evaluation should measure actual ability to choose the correct channel.

Evidence priority:

- High for existence of Malaysian reporting and verification tools.
- Gap: current report must test whether participants can use these tools under scenario pressure.

## Crosswalk to Current Report

| Current report element | Audit interpretation | Required strengthening later |

| Chapter 2 behavioural list: overconfidence, social influence, authority bias, urgency response, emotional attachment, familiarity bias, low self-efficacy | Valid starting point but too susceptibility-focused | Convert into revictimization categories and define repeat-victim mechanisms |
| TPB, PMT, Cognitive Appraisal integration | Conceptually suitable | Map every factor to measurable constructs and scenario outcomes |
| Five intervention modules | Plausible but not yet evidence-weighted | Tie each module to the strongest Malaysia evidence and factor gaps |
| Scenario-based assessment | Strong methodological choice | Scenarios must include local authority, investment, APK, e-commerce, love/recovery scam patterns |
| Revictimization outcome | Currently underdefined | Define as repeated victimization, repeat scam exposure with risky compliance, and post-victim recovery vulnerability |

## Minimum Measurement Rules for the Later Study

1. Separate prior victims, attempted-scam exposed non-victims, and no-known-exposure respondents.
2. Measure actual previous victimization and repeat victimization, not only perceived tendency.
3. Include scenario tasks for all major Malaysian scam channels: authority call, phishing/smishing, investment scam, e-commerce/job scam, love scam, malicious APK, and recovery scam.
4. Score safe behaviour separately from confidence and intention.
5. Include a tool-use capability score covering Semak Mule, NSRC 997, bank hotline, Cyber999, and SC Investment Checker.
6. Include shame/help-seeking items for prior victims.
7. Treat vendor and news evidence as context unless it confirms or extends official/regulatory data.
