## High-Level Report Structure

The template is arranged as a research report / thesis-style document with:

1. Preliminary pages.
2. Main body chapters.
3. References.
4. Appendices.

The main template uses six chapters:

1. Chapter 1: Introduction
2. Chapter 2: Literature Review
3. Chapter 3: Research Methodology
4. Chapter 4: Research Design and Implementation
5. Chapter 5: Results, Analysis and Discussion
6. Chapter 6: Conclusion

The older UTM guideline also mentions a conventional thesis structure where
Chapter 4 may be Results, Chapter 5 Discussion, and Chapter 6 Conclusion. For
this workspace, prefer the six-chapter template above unless the user says
otherwise.

## Preliminary Pages

Expected preliminary order:

1. Front cover.
2. Declaration of thesis / undergraduate project report and copyright form.
3. Title page.
4. Declaration page.
5. Dedication page, optional.
6. Acknowledgement, optional.
7. Abstract.
8. Abstrak.
9. Table of contents.
10. List of tables.
11. List of figures.
12. List of abbreviations.
13. List of symbols.
14. List of appendices.

Notes:

- The title page is counted as Roman page `i`, but the page number does not
  appear.
- Preliminary pages use lower-case Roman numerals.
- The main body starts with Arabic page number `1`.
- Optional pages still affect Roman pagination when included.

## Template Chapter Map

### Chapter 1: Introduction

Use Chapter 1 to establish the project and its academic purpose.

Template sections:

- `1.1 Introduction`
- `1.2 Problem Background`
- `1.3 Research Aim`
- `1.4 Research Question`
- `1.5 Research Objectives`
- `1.6 Research Scope`
- `1.7 Research Contribution`
- `1.8 Report Organization`

Writing expectations:

- Move from broad context to the specific problem.
- Explain why the topic matters.
- State the aim in one clear sentence.
- Keep research questions aligned with objectives.
- Keep objectives measurable and directly answerable.
- Keep the scope realistic and bounded.
- End by explaining how the report is organized.

### Chapter 2: Literature Review

Use Chapter 2 to build the research foundation and justify the proposed
direction.

Template sections:

- `2.1 Introduction`
- `2.2 Problem Formulation`
- `2.2.1 Research Domain`
- `2.2.2 Description of Related Studies`
- `2.3 Proposed Solutions`
- `2.4 Chapter Summary`

Writing expectations:

- Review the domain from general to specific.
- Compare related studies instead of listing them one by one.
- Identify limitations, gaps, and unresolved issues.
- Show how the proposed solution is supported by literature.
- Make every reviewed method, theory, or algorithm relevant to the problem.

### Chapter 3: Research Methodology

Use Chapter 3 to explain how the research is conducted.

Template sections:

- `3.1 Introduction`
- `3.2 Operational Framework/Research Workflow`
- `3.3 Justification`
- `3.4 Performance measurement`
- `3.5 Chapter Summary`

Writing expectations:

- Describe the workflow or operational framework clearly.
- Explain data, tools, methods, processes, and evaluation steps.
- Justify why the selected method is suitable.
- Define performance measures before presenting results.
- Keep methodology reproducible.

### Chapter 4: Research Design and Implementation

Use Chapter 4 to explain the designed solution and how it was implemented.

Template sections:

- `4.1 Introduction`
- `4.2 Proposed Solution`
- `4.3 Experiment Design`
- `4.4 Parameter and Testing Method`
- `4.5 Chapter Summary`

Writing expectations:

- Explain the architecture, model, system, prototype, or design.
- Describe implementation decisions, components, and tools.
- State the experiment setup and testing method.
- Identify important parameters and why they were chosen.
- Keep design details tied to the research objectives.

### Chapter 5: Results, Analysis and Discussion

Use Chapter 5 to present and interpret findings.

Template sections:

- `5.1 Introduction`
- `5.2 Research Results and Analysis`
- `5.3 Future Works`

Writing expectations:

- Present results in a logical order.
- Use tables and figures for data that benefits from comparison.
- Do not repeat full table contents in prose.
- Interpret what the result means.
- Connect findings back to objectives, questions, and literature.
- Discuss limitations honestly.

### Chapter 6: Conclusion

Use Chapter 6 to close the research argument.

Template sections:

- `6.1 Introduction`
- `6.2 Achievement of Project Objectives`
- `6.3 Suggestions for Improvement and Future Works`

Writing expectations:

- Summarize the work without introducing new results.
- State how each objective was achieved.
- Explain the contribution.
- Give realistic future work and improvement suggestions.

## References

The template includes a `REFERENCES` section after Chapter 6.

The example reference list uses an author-date style similar to Harvard / APA
practice. Keep one citation style consistent throughout the whole report.

Basic rule:

- Every in-text citation must appear in the reference list.
- Every reference list entry should be cited in the text.
- Do not mix Harvard, Vancouver, and APA styles in the same report.
- Keep punctuation, author order, capitalization, journal names, volume, issue,
  pages, DOI, and access dates consistent.

The UTM guideline allows Harvard, Vancouver, or APA, but consistency matters more
than the chosen style once the user or supervisor has selected one.

## Appendices

Appendices come after references.

Template examples:

- `Appendix A Mathematical Proofs`
- `Appendix B Psuedo Code`
- `Appendix C Time-series Results Long`

Use appendices for supporting material that is too long or disruptive for the
main text, such as:

- detailed derivations
- long tables
- questionnaires
- source code or pseudo-code
- additional figures
- raw or extended results

Appendices are optional, but if used they must be listed in the List of
Appendices.

## General Formatting Rules

### Language

- The thesis/report may be written in English, Bahasa Melayu, or Arabic where
  applicable.
- Use spelling consistently, for example British English or American English,
  not both.
- Use the Roman alphabet unless the discipline requires otherwise.

### Title Rules

The title should:

- be clear and concise
- describe the focus and contribution of the research
- contain no more than 15 meaningful words, excluding articles, conjunctions,
  and prepositions
- avoid vague research-exercise phrases such as:
  - `An investigation of ...`
  - `A preliminary study of ...`
  - `A study of ...`
  - `Analysis of ...`
  - `On the ...`
  - `Theory of ...`
  - `Some ...`
  - `Toward a ...`
- avoid formulas, symbols, subscripts, Greek letters, and non-alphabetical
  symbols
- avoid acronyms unless they are very common in the field
- avoid punctuation such as colons and semicolons, except commas when necessary

### Page Limits

Maximum page counts from the guideline:

| Document type | Maximum pages |
| --- | ---: |
| Bachelor degree project report | 100 |
| Master's report / dissertation / thesis | 200 |
| Doctorate thesis | 300 |

These limits include text, tables, figures, and illustrations, but exclude
appendices.

### Paper and Layout

- Use portrait layout for normal text.
- Use landscape layout only when necessary for wide tables or figures.
- A4 is used for viva-voce / examination copies.
- B5 is used for final bound copies.
- Use white 80 gsm paper.

### Font

Guideline:

- Use one font family consistently throughout the report.
- Times New Roman, Tahoma, Arial, or Book Antiqua are acceptable.
- If using Microsoft Word, Times New Roman 12 pt or larger is recommended.
- Use 1.5 line spacing for normal text.

Template observation:

- The DOCX has many custom styles. Reuse those styles instead of manually
  formatting paragraphs.

### Margins

Guideline margins:

- left: 3.25 cm
- right: 3.25 cm
- top: 2.5 cm
- bottom: 2.5 cm

Template observation:

- Normal portrait report sections use A4 size with approximately the same
  guideline margins.
- The template contains at least one landscape section for wide tables/figures.

### Spacing and Paragraphs

Guideline spacing rules:

- Between chapter number and chapter title: three `SHIFT+ENTER` line breaks.
- Between chapter title and first text line: four line spacing.
- Between last text line and next subsection title: four line spacing.
- Between subsection title and first text line: two line spacing.
- Between paragraphs: two line spacing.
- Between text and a table, figure, or illustration: two line spacing.
- After a comma: one character space.
- First line of each paragraph should be indented by 1.27 cm.
- Do not begin a new paragraph on the last line of a page.
- Subsection number and title align with the left margin.

### Pagination

- Count every page.
- Print page numbers at the bottom center, 1.25 cm from the bottom edge.
- Preliminary pages use lower-case Roman numerals.
- Main text uses Arabic numerals.
- The title page is counted as `i` but the page number is not printed.
- Chapter pages should start on odd pages where required.
- If a chapter ends on an odd page, insert a blank page; count it but do not
  print its page number.

## Front Matter Details

### Declaration of Thesis / Copyright Form

The form declares whether the thesis is:

- confidential
- restricted
- open access

If confidential or restricted, attach a letter stating:

- reason for classification
- duration of classification

Typical duration is three years. Maximum duration mentioned in the guideline is
five years.

### Title Page

The title page should contain, in order:

1. Thesis/report title.
2. Student full name as in identity card or passport.
3. Statement of award.
4. School name, if applicable.
5. Faculty/school name.
6. University name.
7. Month and year of submission.

Example statement patterns:

- Bachelor project report:
  `A report submitted in partial fulfilment of the requirements for the award of the degree of Bachelor of ...`
- Master's project report:
  `A project report submitted in partial fulfilment of the requirements for the award of the degree of Master of ...`
- Master's dissertation:
  `A dissertation submitted in partial fulfilment of the requirements for the award of the degree of Master of ...`
- Master's thesis by research:
  `A thesis submitted in fulfilment of the requirements for the award of the degree of Master of ...`
- Doctor of Philosophy thesis:
  `A thesis submitted in fulfilment of the requirements for the award of the degree of Doctor of Philosophy ...`

### Declaration Page

The declaration page states that the work is original except where cited and that
it has not been accepted for another degree or submitted concurrently elsewhere.
It must include signature, name, and date.

### Dedication

- Optional.
- Keep brief.
- No charts, photos, numbering, or long explanation.
- Should be no more than one paragraph.

### Acknowledgement

- Optional.
- Usually one page.
- Acknowledge supervisors, collaborators, organizations, funding, family, or
  other support where appropriate.

### Abstract and Abstrak

Abstract requirements:

- Starts with a brief theme sentence.
- States the aim or purpose.
- Explains academic and/or practical importance.
- Briefly describes methodology.
- Summarizes main findings.
- States conclusion and contribution.
- Highlights practical or managerial implications where appropriate.
- Must be bilingual.
- If the report is in English, provide English abstract first, followed by
  Bahasa Melayu `Abstrak`.
- If the report is in Bahasa Melayu, provide `Abstrak` first, followed by
  English abstract.
- Write as one paragraph.
- Do not exceed one page.
- Single spacing or 1.5 spacing is acceptable.

### Table of Contents

- Starts on a new page.
- Lists all sections, chapters, and up to four heading levels.
- Wording must match the headings used in the body.
- Update automatically in Word when possible.

### Lists of Tables, Figures, Symbols, Abbreviations, and Appendices

- List all tables with table number, title, and page.
- List all figures with figure number, title, and page.
- Figures include diagrams, photographs, drawings, graphs, charts, and maps.
- List symbols, abbreviations, notations, and terminology alphabetically where
  applicable.
- List all appendices with appendix letter, title, and page.

## Body Writing Rules

### Chapter and Heading Levels

The report must be divided into chapters and sections.

Use no more than four numbered heading levels:

```text
2       First level: chapter title
2.1     Second level: section
2.1.1   Third level: sub-section
2.1.1.1 Fourth level: sub-sub-section
```

Avoid deeper numbering. If more detail is needed, use letters or roman numerals
after the fourth level.

### Paragraphs

- Write in paragraphs, not loose notes.
- Avoid very long paragraphs.
- Each paragraph should cover one issue or idea.
- Maintain continuity between paragraphs.
- Keep claims linked to evidence, methods, or findings.

### Citations

- Use citation to acknowledge sources and avoid plagiarism.
- Choose one citation style and apply it consistently.
- Reference managers such as Mendeley, EndNote, RefWorks, or JabRef are
  acceptable.
- The final reference list must match the citations used in the text.

### Direct Quotations

- Put long direct quotations in a separate paragraph.
- Use single spacing.
- Do not use quotation marks for indented block quotations.
- Indent left and right by 1.27 cm.
- If the quotation is in another language, use italics.
- Include author, year, and page number according to the chosen citation style.

### Tables

Rules:

- Number tables by chapter, for example `Table 4.3`.
- Refer to every table in the text.
- Place the table after the first reference or as close as possible.
- Put the caption above the table.
- Center a one-line caption.
- Left-align or left-indent a caption that runs over more than one line.
- Repeat header rows when a table continues across pages.
- Put table sources and notes directly below the table.
- Use `Adapted from ...` when a table is adapted from a source.
- Smaller font may be used inside tables if needed.
- Landscape tables are allowed when necessary.

Writing around tables:

- Do not repeat every value in prose.
- Highlight trends, comparisons, and important findings.
- Explain why the table matters.

### Figures

Rules:

- Figures include maps, charts, graphs, drawings, diagrams, and photographs.
- Number figures by chapter, for example `Figure 4.3`.
- Refer to every figure in the text.
- Place the figure after the first reference or as close as possible.
- Put the caption below the figure.
- Center a one-line caption.
- Left-align or left-indent a caption that runs over more than one line.
- Use clear, high-quality images.
- Keep figures within margin requirements.
- Landscape figures are allowed when necessary.

Writing around figures:

- Explain what the reader should notice.
- Do not merely restate the caption.
- Discuss the relevance of the figure to the argument or result.

### Equations

Rules:

- Treat equations as part of the text.
- Number equations by chapter, for example `(2.1)`.
- Center displayed equations.
- Place equation number at the right side of the page.
- Use one number for a multi-line equation.
- Put detailed derivations in an appendix when they interrupt the flow.
- Italicize mathematical parameters, but not units, numbers, or functions such as
  logarithms.

### Footnotes

- Footnotes and endnotes are generally not allowed for Science and Technology
  theses.
- Limited footnotes may be used in Humanities and Social Sciences.
- Footnote text should be two points smaller than body text.

## Reference Style Notes

The guideline mentions three acceptable systems:

- Harvard: author-date in text, alphabetical reference list.
- Vancouver: numbered citations, numerical reference list.
- APA: commonly used in social sciences.

For the template currently provided, use an author-date reference style unless
the user or supervisor asks for Vancouver or another style.

Harvard-style examples of format:

```text
Book:
Author, A. (Year) Title of book. Place: Publisher.

Journal article:
Author, A. and Author, B. (Year) 'Article title', Journal Title, volume(issue), pages. doi: ...

Conference article:
Author, A. (Year) Title of article. Name of conference. Date. Place: Publisher, pages.

Thesis:
Author, A. (Year) Title of thesis. Degree thesis, Institution, Place.
```

## Practical Writing Checklist

Before writing:

- Confirm the report title follows the title rules.
- Confirm which citation style is required.
- Confirm whether the work is a bachelor project report, master's report,
  dissertation, thesis, or PhD thesis.
- Confirm the exact chapter structure requested by the user/supervisor.
- Use the provided template chapter map unless instructed otherwise.

While writing:

- Keep heading text consistent with the table of contents.
- Keep each section aligned with research aim, questions, and objectives.
- Refer to every table and figure in the text.
- Use captions in the correct position.
- Keep citations attached to factual claims from sources.
- Keep methods reproducible.
- Interpret results instead of only presenting them.

Before final delivery:

- Update table of contents.
- Update list of tables.
- Update list of figures.
- Update list of abbreviations, symbols, and appendices.
- Check page numbering.
- Check margins and section orientation.
- Check that references and in-text citations match.
- Remove unused sample content from the template.
- Check abstract and `Abstrak` are both present if required.
- Check title page, declaration, and other front matter names/dates.
