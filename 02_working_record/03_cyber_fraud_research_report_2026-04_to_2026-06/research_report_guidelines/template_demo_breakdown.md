# DOCX Demo Template Breakdown

This file explains the demo template in an easy top-to-bottom way.

Source file:

```text
paper writing guidelines/Template_Tesis_UTM_PSM_UG_SC_Research.docx
```

Use this file when you want to understand the *pattern* of the template:

- what comes first
- what each section is supposed to do
- how much information is usually enough
- where tables, figures, quotations, equations, references, and appendices fit
- how to write in the same academic style without copying the sample words

Do not copy the demo topic, sample student name, sample references, or filler
paragraphs. The DOCX is a style and structure model, not a content source.

## How To Use This File

Read this file before writing or editing the actual report.

Use it together with:

- `thesis_guide.md` for rules and formatting requirements
- the DOCX template for exact Word styles and generated lists
- the actual report draft for the real topic, content, and evidence

Think of this file as the "template logic" in plain English.

## Overall Template Pattern

The template is built like a complete academic report:

1. Cover and administrative pages.
2. Declaration and optional personal pages.
3. Abstract pages.
4. Generated contents and lists.
5. Six main chapters.
6. References.
7. Appendices.

The template does not only show headings. It also shows the expected writing
rhythm:

- introduce each chapter
- explain the problem or context
- provide numbered objectives or scope points where needed
- use literature and evidence in Chapter 2
- use workflow, design, testing, and measurement in Chapters 3 and 4
- present and interpret findings in Chapter 5
- close with objective achievement and future work in Chapter 6

## Important Style Principle

The template is formal, direct, and research-focused.

The writing should:

- be clear and plain
- avoid casual language
- avoid unsupported claims
- connect every section to the research aim and objectives
- explain why tables and figures matter
- avoid decorative or unnecessary content
- avoid copying filler text from the template

Good report writing here is not about sounding complicated. It is about being
complete, precise, and easy for an examiner to follow.

## Top-To-Bottom Template Map

## 1. Cover Page

### What the template shows

The first page uses repeated lines of the title in capital letters, followed by
the student name and university identity.

### What to write

Use:

- full report title in capital letters
- student full name
- university name
- any required school/faculty/program details if required by the final format

### Writing and layout pattern

- The title is the main visual focus.
- The page is not for explanation.
- Do not include abstract, objectives, or background here.
- Keep the title clean and formal.

### What to avoid

- Do not use a long title with punctuation-heavy phrasing.
- Do not add decorative text.
- Do not keep the sample student name.

## 2. Declaration Of Thesis / Copyright Form

### What the template shows

The DOCX includes the UTM declaration/copyright form area.

### What to write

This page is administrative. It normally includes:

- author's full name
- date of birth
- report/thesis title
- academic session
- access status, such as confidential, restricted, or open access
- signatures and official details where required

### Writing and layout pattern

- Use exact institutional wording if required.
- Do not rewrite this page creatively.
- Fill only the required fields.

### What to avoid

- Do not summarize the research here.
- Do not remove official declaration language unless instructed.

## 3. Title Page

### What the template shows

The title page repeats the student name and includes the award statement.

Example pattern:

```text
A thesis submitted in fulfilment of the
requirements for the award of the degree of
...
```

### What to write

Include:

- report title
- student full name
- correct award/submission statement
- program or degree name
- school/faculty
- Universiti Teknologi Malaysia
- month and year of submission

### Writing and layout pattern

- Formal and minimal.
- No discussion or explanation.
- The award statement must match the actual program level.

### What to avoid

- Do not use the wrong degree type.
- Do not leave generic placeholders like `specialisation`.

## 4. Declaration Page

### What the template shows

The declaration page states that the work is original except where cited and has
not been submitted for another degree.

It ends with a small signature table:

- signature
- name
- date

### What to write

Use the required declaration wording, then fill:

- student name
- date
- signature field, if preparing the final DOCX

### Writing and layout pattern

- One formal paragraph is enough.
- This is not a chapter and should not contain research content.

### What to avoid

- Do not change the legal/academic meaning of the declaration.
- Do not keep the sample thesis title.

## 5. Dedication

### What the template shows

A short dedication page.

### What to write

If included, write a short dedication to family, mentors, or someone meaningful.

### Recommended amount

- 1 short paragraph
- no bullets
- no table
- no image

### What to avoid

- Do not make it long or emotional beyond academic tone.
- Do not include research explanation.

## 6. Acknowledgement

### What the template shows

A formal acknowledgement page.

### What to write

Usually thank:

- supervisor
- co-supervisor, if any
- lecturers or examiners, if appropriate
- institution, lab, faculty, or funding body
- participants, collaborators, or data providers
- family or personal support, if appropriate

### Recommended amount

- about half a page to one page
- formal paragraphs

### Writing pattern

Move from academic/professional support first, then personal support.

### What to avoid

- Do not overdo personal storytelling.
- Do not use casual language.

## 7. Abstract

### What the template shows

The abstract appears before `Abstrak`.

### What to write

The abstract should cover, in one compact paragraph:

1. research context or issue
2. problem or gap
3. aim or purpose
4. method or approach
5. main results
6. contribution or implication

### Recommended amount

- one paragraph
- normally one page maximum
- no bullets
- no citations unless specifically required

### Writing pattern

The abstract should read like a miniature version of the whole report.

### What to avoid

- Do not write it as an introduction only.
- Do not include background without findings.
- Do not use unexplained acronyms.
- Do not mention results vaguely if results are available.

## 8. Abstrak

### What the template shows

The Malay abstract follows the English abstract.

### What to write

Write a Bahasa Melayu version of the abstract.

### Recommended amount

- same meaning as the English abstract
- one paragraph
- one page maximum

### What to avoid

- Do not make it shorter in a way that loses meaning.
- Do not machine-translate blindly without checking technical terms.

## 9. Table Of Contents

### What the template shows

The table of contents lists:

- preliminary pages
- Chapter 1 to Chapter 6
- sections and sub-sections
- references

### What to write

In the final DOCX, this should be generated or updated from Word headings.

### Pattern to follow

The template includes up to third and fourth level headings in the contents.
Only include useful levels. Do not overload the contents with tiny subpoints.

### What to avoid

- Do not manually type page numbers if the DOCX can generate them.
- Do not let headings in the body differ from the contents.

## 10. List Of Tables

### What the template shows

The list has columns:

```text
TABLE NO. | TITLE | PAGE
```

### What to write

List every table used in the report.

### Pattern to follow

Table numbering follows chapters:

```text
Table 2.1
Table 2.2
Table 4.1
Table A.1
```

### What to avoid

- Do not include a table in the list if it is not in the report.
- Do not use inconsistent captions.

## 11. List Of Figures

### What the template shows

The list has columns:

```text
FIGURE NO. | TITLE | PAGE
```

### What to write

List every figure, diagram, chart, graph, or image used in the report.

### Pattern to follow

Figure numbering follows chapters:

```text
Figure 2.1
Figure 3.1
Figure A.1
```

### What to avoid

- Do not call a visual "figure" in one place and "diagram" in another caption
  format.
- Do not include unclear or low-quality visuals.

## 12. List Of Abbreviations

### What the template shows

The template uses a simple table:

```text
ANN - Artificial Neural Network
GA  - Genetic Algorithm
PSO - Particle Swarm Optimization
```

### What to write

List abbreviations that appear in the report.

### Pattern to follow

- abbreviation on the left
- dash in the middle
- full phrase on the right
- alphabetical order is preferred

### What to avoid

- Do not include abbreviations that are never used.
- Do not define the same abbreviation differently in the body.

## 13. List Of Symbols

### What the template shows

The template uses a symbol table with symbol, dash, and meaning.

### What to write

List mathematical or technical symbols used in the report.

### Pattern to follow

- symbol on the left
- dash in the middle
- explanation on the right

### What to avoid

- Do not list standard units unless necessary.
- Do not leave blank symbols from the sample template.

## 14. List Of Appendices

### What the template shows

The list has columns:

```text
APPENDIX | TITLE | PAGE
```

### What to write

List all appendices included after the references.

### Pattern to follow

```text
Appendix A  Mathematical Proofs
Appendix B  Pseudo Code
Appendix C  Time-series Results
```

Use real appendix titles for the actual report.

## 15. Chapter 1: Introduction

Chapter 1 explains the research problem and sets up the whole report.

### 1.1 Introduction

Purpose:

- introduce the topic area
- give broad context
- prepare the reader for the problem background

Recommended amount:

- 2 to 4 paragraphs

Writing pattern:

- start broad
- move toward the specific subject
- avoid going too deep into literature review

### 1.2 Problem Background

Purpose:

- explain the real problem, issue, gap, or motivation
- show why the work is needed
- lead naturally to the aim and questions

Recommended amount:

- 3 to 6 paragraphs

Writing pattern:

- describe current situation
- identify limitation or problem
- show consequence or importance
- narrow toward the report's focus

### 1.3 Research Aim

Purpose:

- state the main goal of the research

Recommended amount:

- 1 clear sentence or 1 short paragraph

Template pattern:

The template explicitly says the research aim shows what the project plans to
achieve in one sentence.

Writing pattern:

```text
The aim of this research is to ...
```

### 1.4 Research Question

Purpose:

- convert the problem into answerable research questions

Recommended amount:

- 1 introductory sentence
- usually 2 to 4 questions

Writing pattern:

Questions should match the research objectives.

### 1.5 Research Objectives

Purpose:

- list measurable steps used to achieve the aim

Recommended amount:

- 1 introductory sentence
- usually 3 to 5 bullet points

Template pattern:

The template uses:

```text
The objectives of the research are:
- objective 1
- objective 2
- objective 3
```

Writing pattern:

Start each objective with an action verb, such as:

- to identify
- to design
- to develop
- to evaluate
- to compare
- to analyse

### 1.6 Research Scope

Purpose:

- define boundaries of the work

Recommended amount:

- 1 introductory sentence
- usually 2 to 5 bullet points

Writing pattern:

Scope points can cover:

- target users or population
- dataset or case study
- tools or platform
- included methods
- excluded work

### 1.7 Research Contribution

Purpose:

- explain what the work adds

Recommended amount:

- 2 to 4 paragraphs or concise points

Writing pattern:

Contributions may be:

- practical contribution
- technical contribution
- methodological contribution
- academic contribution

### 1.8 Report Organization

Purpose:

- tell the reader what each chapter contains

Recommended amount:

- 1 paragraph, or one short paragraph per chapter

Writing pattern:

Briefly mention Chapter 2 through Chapter 6.

What to avoid:

- Do not repeat the full table of contents.
- Do not introduce new findings here.

## 16. Chapter 2: Literature Review

Chapter 2 explains what existing work says and where the research gap is.

### 2.1 Introduction

Purpose:

- introduce the literature review chapter
- explain what areas will be reviewed

Recommended amount:

- 1 to 2 paragraphs

### Figure Placement Pattern In Chapter 2

The template places a figure early in Chapter 2.

Pattern:

- introduce concept in text
- insert figure
- caption below figure
- explain what the figure shows

Use this when a concept, framework, process, or model is easier to understand
visually.

### Table Placement Pattern In Chapter 2

The template places multiple tables in Chapter 2.

Pattern:

- introduce why comparison/data is needed
- insert table
- caption above table
- discuss key points after the table

Use tables for:

- comparing related studies
- summarizing methods
- listing datasets
- showing feature comparison
- showing literature gaps

### 2.2 Problem Formulation

Purpose:

- connect the literature to the specific problem
- explain how the problem is framed academically

Recommended amount:

- 2 to 4 paragraphs

Writing pattern:

- describe domain
- identify problem area
- connect to related studies
- lead into research gap

### 2.2.1 Research Domain

Purpose:

- define and explain the domain or field

Recommended amount:

- 2 to 5 paragraphs

Writing pattern:

- define key terms
- explain major concepts
- cite credible sources
- narrow toward the actual project

### 2.2.2 Description Of Related Studies

Purpose:

- discuss previous work relevant to the project

Recommended amount:

- enough to cover important studies, often several pages

Writing pattern:

- group studies by method/theme/problem, not just author-by-author
- compare strengths and limitations
- make the gap visible

### Quotation Pattern

The template includes a block quotation with a separate reference line.

Use direct quotations rarely.

Only use them when:

- exact wording is important
- a definition or authoritative statement is being preserved

Otherwise, paraphrase and cite.

### Equation Pattern

The template includes an equation in Chapter 2.

Use equations when the research needs:

- formulas
- metrics
- models
- calculations

Pattern:

- introduce the equation
- show the equation centered
- number it by chapter, such as `(2.1)`
- explain every variable
- explain how it is used

### 2.3 Proposed Solutions

Purpose:

- explain theories, algorithms, methods, or approaches that can solve the problem

Recommended amount:

- 3 to 8 paragraphs depending on complexity

Writing pattern:

- describe the chosen method
- explain why it fits the problem
- compare briefly with alternatives
- justify the final direction

### 2.4 Chapter Summary

Purpose:

- summarize the literature review
- restate the gap
- transition to methodology

Recommended amount:

- 1 to 2 paragraphs

What to avoid:

- Do not add new studies in the summary.

## 17. Chapter 3: Research Methodology

Chapter 3 explains how the research will be carried out.

### 3.1 Introduction

Purpose:

- introduce the methodology chapter
- briefly explain what the chapter covers

Recommended amount:

- 1 to 2 paragraphs

### 3.2 Operational Framework / Research Workflow

Purpose:

- show the sequence of research activities
- explain the process from input to output

Recommended amount:

- a diagram or workflow figure if useful
- 3 to 6 paragraphs explaining the flow

Writing pattern:

Explain:

- data/source/input
- preprocessing or preparation
- method/model/development process
- testing or evaluation
- output/result

### 3.3 Justification

Purpose:

- explain why the chosen methodology is suitable

Recommended amount:

- 2 to 4 paragraphs

Writing pattern:

- connect method to objectives
- mention why alternatives were not selected if relevant
- support with literature when possible

### 3.4 Performance Measurement

Purpose:

- define how success will be measured

Recommended amount:

- 2 to 5 paragraphs
- include equations or tables if needed

Examples of performance measurements:

- accuracy
- precision
- recall
- F1-score
- error rate
- response time
- user satisfaction score
- comparison against baseline

### 3.5 Chapter Summary

Purpose:

- summarize methodology
- transition to design and implementation

Recommended amount:

- 1 paragraph

## 18. Chapter 4: Research Design And Implementation

Chapter 4 explains what was built or designed and how it was tested.

### 4.1 Introduction

Purpose:

- introduce design and implementation chapter

Recommended amount:

- 1 to 2 paragraphs

### 4.2 Proposed Solution

Purpose:

- explain the actual solution, system, model, process, prototype, or framework

Recommended amount:

- several paragraphs
- diagrams or architecture figures if useful

Writing pattern:

- describe main components
- explain how components interact
- explain what makes the solution suitable
- link solution back to objectives

### 4.3 Experiment Design

Purpose:

- explain how the solution is tested or evaluated

Recommended amount:

- 2 to 5 paragraphs
- table if there are multiple scenarios, cases, or parameters

Writing pattern:

- describe setup
- describe sample/data/case
- describe test conditions
- describe evaluation procedure

### 4.4 Parameter And Testing Method

Purpose:

- document important parameters and testing details

Recommended amount:

- 2 to 5 paragraphs
- table if there are many parameters

Writing pattern:

- list parameter names
- state values/settings
- explain why they are selected
- state how testing is performed

### Wide Table Pattern

The template includes a wide table in this area.

Use a table when there are many columns or experimental values.

If it is too wide:

- use landscape orientation in the DOCX
- keep caption above the table
- keep page number placement consistent with guideline

### 4.5 Chapter Summary

Purpose:

- summarize design and implementation
- transition to results

Recommended amount:

- 1 paragraph

## 19. Chapter 5: Results, Analysis And Discussion

Chapter 5 presents what happened and what it means.

### 5.1 Introduction

Purpose:

- introduce results chapter
- remind reader what will be evaluated

Recommended amount:

- 1 to 2 paragraphs

### 5.2 Research Results And Analysis

Purpose:

- present results
- analyse findings
- discuss meaning

Recommended amount:

- enough to answer every research objective
- usually the main section of the chapter

Writing pattern:

For each result:

1. introduce what is being evaluated
2. present table or figure if needed
3. describe the key finding
4. interpret why it matters
5. compare with objective, baseline, or literature
6. mention limitations if relevant

Do not only write:

```text
The result is shown in Table 5.1.
```

Instead, explain the important pattern or implication.

### 5.3 Future Works

Purpose:

- identify improvements or extensions

Recommended amount:

- 1 to 3 paragraphs or concise points

Writing pattern:

Future work should be realistic and connected to limitations.

What to avoid:

- Do not use future work to hide missing required work.
- Do not introduce unrelated new project ideas.

## 20. Chapter 6: Conclusion

Chapter 6 closes the report.

### 6.1 Introduction

Purpose:

- briefly introduce the closing chapter

Recommended amount:

- 1 short paragraph

### 6.2 Achievement Of Project Objectives

Purpose:

- show how each objective was achieved

Recommended amount:

- 1 paragraph per objective, or a concise table plus explanation

Writing pattern:

For each objective:

- restate the objective
- state what was done
- state evidence/result that proves achievement

This section is important because examiners often check whether the report
delivered what Chapter 1 promised.

### 6.3 Suggestions For Improvement And Future Works

Purpose:

- give final improvement suggestions

Recommended amount:

- 1 to 3 paragraphs

Writing pattern:

- acknowledge limitations
- suggest specific improvements
- keep suggestions tied to the project

## 21. References

### What the template shows

The template includes an author-date style reference list after Chapter 6.

### What to write

Use real sources cited in the report.

### Pattern to follow

Each reference should be complete and consistent:

- author
- year
- title
- publication/source
- volume/issue/pages if applicable
- DOI or URL if applicable

### Recommended amount

There is no fixed number in the template. The number of references should match
the scope of the literature review and claims made in the report.

### What to avoid

- Do not keep the sample references unless they are actually relevant and cited.
- Do not cite sources in the references that are never mentioned in the report.
- Do not mix citation styles.

## 22. Appendices

### What the template shows

The template includes:

- Appendix A
- Appendix B
- Appendix C

It also includes sample appendix figures and tables.

### What to write

Use appendices for supporting material:

- long proof
- pseudo-code
- raw results
- large tables
- survey forms
- screenshots
- extra implementation detail
- additional charts

### Pattern to follow

Each appendix should:

- start on a new page
- have a clear title
- be listed in the List of Appendices
- contain material referred to in the main text where possible

### What to avoid

- Do not use appendices as a dumping ground.
- Do not include irrelevant sample content.
- Do not include material that should be explained in the main chapters.

## Reusable Writing Rhythm

Most sections in the template follow this rhythm:

1. Start with a short orientation sentence.
2. Explain the concept, issue, method, or result.
3. Add evidence, table, figure, equation, or citation if useful.
4. Explain the meaning.
5. Link back to the research objective or next section.

This rhythm should be repeated across chapters.

## Common Amount Guide

This is not a strict rule, but it helps keep the report balanced.

| Section type | Usual amount |
| --- | --- |
| Chapter introduction | 1 to 2 paragraphs |
| Problem background | 3 to 6 paragraphs |
| Research aim | 1 sentence or 1 short paragraph |
| Research questions | 2 to 4 questions |
| Research objectives | 3 to 5 bullet points |
| Research scope | 2 to 5 bullet points |
| Chapter summary | 1 to 2 paragraphs |
| Abstract | 1 paragraph, max 1 page |
| Acknowledgement | half page to 1 page |
| Dedication | 1 short paragraph |
| Results analysis | as much as needed to answer objectives |

## Table And Figure Style Pattern

Tables:

- caption above
- numbered by chapter
- used for comparison, data, parameters, or summaries
- discussed in text

Figures:

- caption below
- numbered by chapter
- used for concepts, workflow, architecture, charts, or screenshots
- discussed in text

Never insert a table or figure without explaining it.

## How To Replicate The Template Without Copying It

Safe to replicate:

- chapter order
- section order
- paragraph rhythm
- formal academic tone
- use of objective bullets
- use of scope bullets
- caption placement
- table/figure numbering pattern
- references section style
- appendix organization

Do not copy:

- sample student name
- sample title
- sample references
- filler Word text
- example table values
- example figure captions unless relevant
- any topic-specific sentence from the template

## Final Checklist For A Report Based On This Template

Before considering a report complete:

- Cover page has real title and name.
- Title page has correct award statement.
- Declaration page has correct title, name, and date.
- Abstract and Abstrak are present if required.
- Table of contents is updated.
- List of tables is updated.
- List of figures is updated.
- List of abbreviations contains only used abbreviations.
- List of symbols contains only used symbols.
- List of appendices matches actual appendices.
- Chapter 1 clearly states aim, questions, objectives, scope, contribution, and
  report organization.
- Chapter 2 shows literature, comparison, and gap.
- Chapter 3 explains methodology and measurement.
- Chapter 4 explains design, implementation, experiment, and testing method.
- Chapter 5 presents and interprets results.
- Chapter 6 explains objective achievement and future work.
- Every table and figure is referenced in text.
- Every citation appears in references.
- Every reference is cited in text.
- Template filler text is removed.
- All captions and headings are consistent.

## Short Version For Future Agents

If a future agent is in a hurry, remember this:

The DOCX template is a complete academic report pattern. Follow its order and
style, but replace all sample content. The report should move from problem setup
in Chapter 1, to literature and gap in Chapter 2, to methodology in Chapter 3,
to design and testing in Chapter 4, to results and discussion in Chapter 5, and
finally to objective achievement and future work in Chapter 6. Use tables,
figures, equations, citations, and appendices only when they support the
research argument.
