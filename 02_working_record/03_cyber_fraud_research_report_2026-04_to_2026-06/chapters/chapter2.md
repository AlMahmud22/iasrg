CHAPTER 2 LITERATURE REVIEW

2.1 Introduction

This chapter reviews the literature and evidence needed to support a Malaysia-specific study of cyber fraud revictimization. The review begins by formulating the problem through official and regulatory evidence, then explains the research domain, related scam types, Malaysian scam techniques, behavioural theories, and existing prevention approaches. The chapter gives particular attention to the difference between general cyber fraud awareness and revictimization prevention because the study aims to measure repeated vulnerability after prior exposure or victimization.

The literature review is structured around three foundations. First, Malaysian cyber fraud evidence is used to show the local scam environment, including Cyber999/MyCERT reports, Bank Negara Malaysia anti-fraud initiatives, NSRC reporting, Semak Mule, Securities Commission Malaysia investor-protection sources, MCMC cooperation, and relevant PDRM-linked evidence. Second, academic sources are used to justify behavioural constructs such as overconfidence, social influence, self-efficacy, appraisal, and protective intention. Third, the seven-factor revictimization taxonomy is used to convert the literature into measurable constructs and intervention targets.

2.2 Problem Formulation

Cyber fraud continues to affect Malaysians despite stronger banking safeguards, reporting mechanisms, law-enforcement response, platform takedown efforts, and public awareness campaigns. Bank Negara Malaysia reported that the National Fraud Portal was launched in September 2024 and supported faster identification of suspected fraud transactions and mule accounts (Bank Negara Malaysia, 2025). The Ministry of Finance Malaysia reported extensive use of the NSRC, including 122,603 calls from its launch on 12 October 2022 to August 2024 and blocked suspicious transactions in 2023 (Ministry of Finance Malaysia, 2024). CyberSecurity Malaysia reported that fraud accounted for 71 percent of Cyber999 incidents in Q4 2024 and 68 percent in Q1 2025 (CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025a; CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025b).

These figures should not be combined into one simple national trend table because they measure different things. Cyber999 reports are incident reports handled by CyberSecurity Malaysia, NSRC data reflects scam-reporting and advisory calls, BNM indicators reflect banking-system fraud response, PDRM/Semak Mule evidence reflects verification infrastructure, and Securities Commission Malaysia evidence reflects capital-market and investment-scam enforcement. For that reason, this chapter replaces the earlier mixed 2019-2025 case-and-loss table with safer evidence indicators, as shown in Table 2.1.

The key research problem is that institutional controls depend partly on public behaviour. A user must know when to stop, what to verify, which channel to use, and how to report quickly. This is difficult because scams deliberately compress decision time, borrow authority, use local institutional names, exploit social proof, trigger financial hope, and create shame or secrecy. Cyber fraud prevention is therefore not only a matter of technical protection or public messaging. It also requires measurement of behavioural intention, coping confidence, cognitive appraisal, and practical response capability.

Revictimization makes this problem sharper. A prior victim may become more cautious after a scam, but the experience may also create shame, anxiety, avoidance, desperation to recover money, reduced self-confidence, or repeated exposure through leaked contact details. The Malaysian evidence supports studying victimization mechanisms, scam infrastructure, and reporting tools, but direct repeat-victim evidence remains limited. This study therefore treats revictimization as the measurable research gap rather than an already-proven conclusion.

2.2.1 Research Domain

This research is positioned within three connected domains: human-centred cybersecurity, digital financial crime, and behavioural psychology. In human-centred cybersecurity, the public user becomes a major attack surface because fraudsters can bypass technical safeguards through persuasion. A fake authority call, phishing message, malicious APK request, or investment scheme may succeed only after the user interprets the situation as legitimate or urgent and performs the requested action.

In digital financial crime, cyber fraud is connected to payment channels, mule accounts, social media advertisements, e-wallets, bank transfers, fake websites, platform accounts, and cross-channel communication. Securities Commission Malaysia has documented investment-scam tactics involving social media, fake legitimacy, clone entities, mule accounts, Telegram activity, suspicious URLs, e-wallet channels, and crypto-related lures (Securities Commission Malaysia, n.d.; Securities Commission Malaysia, 2022; Securities Commission Malaysia, 2026). PDRM-linked Semak Mule evidence also shows the importance of checking suspicious bank accounts, phone numbers, and company names (Royal Malaysia Police, 2025). Malaysia's wider cybersecurity governance context is also supported by the Cyber Security Act 2024, although that Act should be treated as national cybersecurity governance context rather than direct evidence of consumer scam revictimization (National Cyber Security Agency Malaysia, 2024).

In behavioural psychology, cyber fraud is studied as a decision-making problem under uncertainty. TPB explains protective intention through attitude, subjective norms, and perceived behavioural control (Ajzen, 1991). PMT explains protection motivation through threat appraisal and coping appraisal (Rogers, 1975; Rogers, 1983). Cognitive Appraisal Theory explains how people interpret threats, evaluate coping options, and reappraise stressful events (Lazarus and Folkman, 1984; Folkman et al., 1986). These theories support the study's focus on why people fail to verify, why prior victims may delay reporting, and why some users remain vulnerable despite awareness.

2.2.2 Description of Related Studies

2.2.2.1 Types of Cyber Fraud in Malaysia

Cyber fraud categories in Malaysia overlap across channels and psychological mechanisms. A single incident may combine impersonation, phishing, mule accounts, social-media advertising, malicious links, emotional grooming, and payment redirection. Therefore, this study classifies fraud types by both scam channel and behavioural trigger, as shown in Table 2.2.

The classification shows why the report should not rely only on a generic awareness model. Different scams use different narratives, but they often share the same behavioural mechanisms: overconfidence, social influence, authority, urgency, emotional pressure, financial opportunity, and low protective capability.

2.2.2.2 Techniques and Modus Operandi

Malaysian scam techniques are highly localized. CyberSecurity Malaysia's Q1 2025 report identified themes such as government aid scams, fake promotions or discounts, traffic summons scams, subscription impersonation, smishing involving banks, e-wallets and delivery services, and vishing by parties impersonating police, LHDN, MCMC, banks, companies, or CyberSecurity Malaysia (CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025b). These cues are important because a message that names a familiar local agency or everyday service is more likely to be appraised as legitimate.

Investment scams use a different but related technique. Securities Commission Malaysia warns that scammers promote high returns with little or no risk, use social media and messaging platforms, create fake testimonials, impersonate licensed entities, use celebrity or prominent-personality endorsements, and request deposits into personal or unrelated accounts (Securities Commission Malaysia, n.d.). SC clone-firm findings also show how social media advertisements, agents, WhatsApp chats, mule accounts, and false legitimacy can be combined in a single investment scam (Securities Commission Malaysia, 2022).

Malicious APK fraud demonstrates the link between social engineering and technical compromise. CyberSecurity Malaysia reported malicious APK themes in Q4 2024 and Q1 2025, and Kaspersky (2025) described suspicious or malicious APK samples in Malaysia and Brunei where users were persuaded to install applications through messenger-style bait. This evidence is useful for scenario design, but vendor threat intelligence should not be treated as national prevalence data.

2.2.2.3 Seven Revictimization Factor Categories

The seven-factor taxonomy is the main behavioural backbone of this study. It upgrades the earlier generic susceptibility discussion into a revictimization-focused model that can be measured in the questionnaire, tested through scenarios, and translated into intervention modules.

| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

| F7 Protective self-efficacy, verification friction, and recovery/reporting capability | The user does not know what to do, doubts action effectiveness, or perceives reporting as difficult | Bank Negara Malaysia (2025, 2026a, 2026b); CyberSecurity Malaysia (2026); Royal Malaysia Police (2025); Securities Commission Malaysia (n.d.) | PMT self-efficacy/response efficacy; TPB perceived behavioural control | Test ability to choose and use correct verification/reporting channels |

F1 is important because awareness does not automatically become safe action. Balakrishnan et al. (2025) found that overconfidence significantly predicted online fraud victimization among Malaysian adults. This supports the study's decision to measure awareness separately from behaviour. A respondent who says they know about scams may still click a realistic link or skip verification in a scenario.

F2 shows that social influence can be harmful or protective depending on the source. Scam social proof includes fake testimonials, agents, group chats, familiar contacts, celebrity images, and apparent peer approval. Genuine protective social support, in contrast, can help users verify suspicious messages. The study must distinguish these two forms of social influence.

F3 and F4 are central to authority and urgency scams. Local scam narratives often use enforcement, tax, bank, regulator, court, account suspension, or legal-action claims. When authority and urgency occur together, the user may appraise the situation as dangerous and immediate, reducing the likelihood of independent verification.

F5 is the most revictimization-specific factor. Emotional grooming, shame, self-blame, reporting reluctance, recovery-scam offers, and repeated contact after a first loss must be measured directly. SC clone-firm findings that many identified victims declined to cooperate with investigation can support the relevance of cooperation friction, but the study must not assume the reason without measuring shame or perceived recovery value (Securities Commission Malaysia, 2022).

F6 captures financial pressure and opportunity bias. Investment and job scams are not only information problems; they exploit financial need, fast-profit hope, task escalation, fees, tax claims, and fear of missing out. This factor requires investment, job, e-commerce, loan, e-wallet, and crypto-related scenarios.

F7 captures protective capability. Users may know the name of NSRC, Semak Mule, Cyber999, a bank hotline, or SC Investment Checker, but still fail to choose the correct channel under pressure. The intervention must therefore teach and test tool-use capability, not only tool awareness.

2.2.2.4 Theoretical Perspectives

The Theory of Planned Behavior explains how protective behaviour is shaped by intention, attitude, subjective norms, and perceived behavioural control (Ajzen, 1991). In this study, attitude refers to whether verification, reporting, refusal, and help-seeking are viewed as necessary and worthwhile. Subjective norms refer to whether family, friends, community, or institutions support safe behaviour. Perceived behavioural control refers to whether a person believes they can perform protective action.

Protection Motivation Theory explains why threat awareness alone is insufficient. People need to believe that cyber fraud is serious and personally relevant, but they also need response efficacy and self-efficacy. If a person believes a threat is severe but does not know what to do, fear may produce panic, avoidance, or compliance instead of protection (Rogers, 1975; Rogers, 1983).

Cognitive Appraisal Theory explains the real-time interpretation of scam situations. Primary appraisal concerns whether the situation is seen as harmless, beneficial, threatening, suspicious, or urgent. Secondary appraisal concerns whether the person believes they have coping options. Reappraisal occurs when new information changes interpretation (Lazarus and Folkman, 1984; Folkman et al., 1986). This theory is especially relevant because scam interactions often occur under emotion, pressure, uncertainty, and incomplete information.

| --- | --- | --- | --- |

2.2.2.5 Revictimization in Cyber Fraud

Revictimization in this study refers to repeated vulnerability after prior cyber fraud exposure or confirmed victimization. It includes repeat financial loss, repeated risky compliance after previous scam exposure, repeated contact by fraudsters, exposure to recovery scams, delayed reporting after loss, and continued vulnerability caused by shame or low self-efficacy.

The literature and evidence base does not yet prove a complete Malaysian repeat-victim model. Instead, it provides enough support to justify measuring the gap. Balakrishnan et al. (2025) supports behavioural predictors of online fraud victimization. Alavi et al. (2018) supports emotional grooming mechanisms in Malaysian love scams. SC and PDRM/Bernama evidence supports clone-firm deception, victim cooperation friction, fake NSRC accounts, rapid reporting needs, and mule-account ecosystems (Securities Commission Malaysia, 2022; Bernama, 2025a; Bernama, 2025b). These sources justify direct measurement of prior-victim recovery, shame, repeated targeting, and recovery-scam vulnerability.

2.3 Proposed Solutions

2.3.1 Existing Approaches

Malaysia's existing cyber fraud responses include banking controls, NSRC, National Fraud Portal, Semak Mule, Cyber999/MyCERT, SC Investment Checker and Investor Alert List, MCMC cooperation, PDRM reporting, and public awareness materials. These approaches are necessary because cyber fraud is a national digital-trust and financial-safety issue. Table 2.6 summarizes their behavioural relevance.

Table 2.6: Existing Cyber Fraud Prevention Approaches and Behavioural Relevance

| Approach | Function | Behavioural value | Limitation for revictimization |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| NSRC 997 and bank hotlines | Rapid response for active scam transfers | Encourages fast reporting and fund-blocking attempts | Requires victims to know and act quickly |
| National Fraud Portal | Supports faster fraud-transaction identification and mule-account identification | Strengthens system response after reports | Does not replace individual verification |
| Semak Mule | Checks suspicious bank accounts, phone numbers, and company names | Supports pre-transfer verification | Users must know when and how to use it |
| Cyber999/MyCERT | National point of contact for computer security incidents | Supports cyber-incident reporting and advisories | Broader than financial scam reporting |
| SC Investment Checker and Investor Alert List | Helps verify investment entities and warnings | Supports investment-scam prevention | Specific to capital-market and investment context |
| MCMC and regulator cooperation | Supports takedown or blocking of scam content | Reduces exposure to known infrastructure | Cannot remove all personalised or new scam attempts |
| Public awareness campaigns | Explain scam types and warnings | Builds general knowledge | May not change behaviour under fear, pressure, shame, or opportunity bias |

2.3.2 Limitations of Existing Approaches

Existing approaches are limited when the user voluntarily performs an unsafe action after deception. A warning, hotline, portal, or reporting system can help only if the person pauses, verifies, refuses unsafe instructions, and reports through the correct channel. PDRM's public advisory on NSRC described a multi-agency response involving NFCC, PDRM, BNM, MCMC, banks, and telcos, but the public still needs to recognise when and how to contact the correct channel (Royal Malaysia Police, 2022). Scammers design messages to prevent exactly these behaviours by creating urgency, authority, secrecy, social proof, emotional dependency, and reward expectation.

General awareness is also limited. Balakrishnan et al. (2025) showed that overconfidence and social influence predicted online fraud victimization among Malaysian adults. This means that a person may know about scams but still fail when faced with a realistic, emotionally charged, or socially endorsed scam. Intervention must therefore include behavioural rehearsal and scenario-based response training.

Prior victims may need different support from the general public. A previous loss may produce shame, self-blame, avoidance, low self-efficacy, or desperate recovery-seeking. The warning by PDRM through Bernama that fake NSRC Facebook accounts were detected is important because it shows that even help or recovery channels can be impersonated (Bernama, 2025a). A revictimization intervention must therefore include recovery-scam warnings, evidence preservation, reporting confidence, and shame reduction.

2.3.3 Theory-Based Proposed Direction

The proposed direction is a theory-based behavioural intervention that moves from general awareness to measurable protective capability. TPB guides the formation of protective intention. PMT guides threat and coping appraisal. Cognitive Appraisal Theory guides recognition and reappraisal of scam cues. The seven-factor taxonomy translates these theories into Malaysia-specific constructs, scenarios, and module outcomes.

The intervention should include five module areas: cyber fraud and revictimization awareness; scam cue detection and cognitive reappraisal; threat and coping appraisal strengthening; verification, reporting, and protective action routines; and recovery, shame reduction, social support, and revictimization resistance. These modules should be evaluated using pre-test and post-test measures, scenario performance, tool-use capability, and recovery-scam resistance.

2.4 Chapter Summary

This chapter reviewed Malaysian cyber fraud as a behavioural, institutional, and digital-security problem. Official and regulatory evidence shows active local scam response systems, Cyber999 fraud incident patterns, NSRC reporting, National Fraud Portal development, Semak Mule verification, investment scam enforcement, and regulator-platform cooperation. Academic evidence supports the importance of overconfidence, social influence, emotional grooming, and behavioural decision-making.

The chapter established the seven-factor taxonomy as the main research spine for the study. The review also showed that the strongest evidence supports scam mechanisms and victimization risk, while direct repeat-victim evidence in Malaysia remains limited. Therefore, revictimization must be measured directly through respondent grouping, prior-victim questions, scenario performance, reporting behaviour, and recovery-scam resistance. The next chapter explains the methodology used to conduct this measurement and evaluate the intervention modules.
