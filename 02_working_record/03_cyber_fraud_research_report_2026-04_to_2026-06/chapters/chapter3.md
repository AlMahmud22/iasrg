CHAPTER 3 RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

3.1 Introduction

This chapter explains the methodology used to investigate cyber fraud revictimization among the public in Malaysia and to evaluate theory-based intervention modules. The methodology is designed to answer the research questions in Chapter 1 and to operationalise the seven-factor taxonomy developed in Chapter 2. The study uses a quantitative survey supported by scenario-based assessment and a pre-test/post-test intervention evaluation.

The methodology is built on three principles. First, revictimization must be measured directly instead of inferred from general scam awareness. Second, awareness, intention, confidence, and actual scenario response must be measured separately. Third, Malaysian scam evidence must be reflected in the questionnaire, scenario tasks, reporting-tool items, and intervention activities. These principles ensure that the study is methodologically workable and aligned with the research aim.

3.2 Operational Framework / Research Workflow

The research workflow consists of two main phases. Phase One identifies psychosocial factors associated with cyber fraud revictimization. Phase Two develops and evaluates intervention modules designed to reduce revictimization tendency and strengthen protective behaviour. The workflow begins with literature review and evidence mapping, then moves to instrument design, expert review, pilot testing, data collection, statistical analysis, intervention design, and intervention evaluation.

Table 3.1: Research Phases and Outputs

| Phase | Activity | Output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Phase 1 | Review cyber fraud, revictimization, Malaysian scam evidence, TPB, PMT, and Cognitive Appraisal Theory | Research gap and theoretical foundation |
| Phase 2 | Map the seven revictimization factors to constructs, Malaysian cues, and outcomes | Factor-to-measurement matrix |
| Phase 3 | Develop questionnaire, prior-victim items, tool-use items, and scenario tasks | Draft research instrument |
| Phase 4 | Conduct expert review and pilot test | Refined instrument and clearer items |
| Phase 5 | Collect survey data from Malaysian internet users, with intentional prior-victim recruitment | Dataset for factor and subgroup analysis |
| Phase 6 | Conduct reliability, validity, descriptive, relationship, and scenario-performance analysis | Evidence on predictors of revictimization tendency |
| Phase 7 | Implement five intervention modules | Theory-based revictimization prevention programme |
| Phase 8 | Conduct pre-test/post-test evaluation and optional delayed follow-up | Evidence of module effectiveness |

The study uses a sequential logic. The literature review identifies the factors and the Malaysia-specific scam cues. The questionnaire measures the constructs and classifies respondent experience. Scenario tasks test whether respondents can apply safe responses in realistic situations. The intervention modules then target the factor gaps identified by the literature and measurement model. Finally, module effectiveness is evaluated using pre-test and post-test comparisons.

3.2.1 Research Design

This study adopts a quantitative research design with an intervention evaluation component. The quantitative approach is suitable because the study measures latent constructs such as attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioural control, perceived severity, perceived vulnerability, response efficacy, self-efficacy, response cost, cognitive appraisal, protective intention, shame, reporting intention, and revictimization tendency. Quantitative survey design is also suitable for examining relationships between constructs and comparing respondent subgroups (Creswell and Creswell, 2018).

The study has two connected designs. The first is a cross-sectional survey that identifies factors associated with cyber fraud revictimization tendency. The second is a pre-test/post-test intervention evaluation that measures whether the proposed modules improve scam detection, protective self-efficacy, response efficacy, reporting intention, tool-use capability, scenario-based safe response, and recovery-scam resistance. If time and access permit, a delayed follow-up test will be added to examine whether improvement is retained after two to four weeks.

3.2.2 Respondent Groups and Sampling Strategy

The target population is Malaysian internet users who use digital communication platforms, online banking, e-commerce, social media, e-wallets, mobile applications, or messaging services. The study will include respondents with different levels of scam exposure because revictimization cannot be measured if all respondents are treated as one general public group.

Respondents will be classified into four groups:

(a) Prior victims: respondents who previously experienced cyber fraud victimization, such as financial loss, credential compromise, malicious installation, unauthorised personal-data misuse, or a reported scam incident.

(b) Repeat victims: respondents who experienced more than one cyber fraud victimization event or a new victimization after an earlier scam incident.

(c) Attempted-scam exposed non-victims: respondents who encountered scam attempts but did not suffer confirmed loss or compromise.

(d) No-known-exposure respondents: respondents who do not recall direct scam exposure or victimization.

The sampling method will use purposive and convenience sampling. Purposive sampling is necessary because the study requires prior victims and attempted-scam targets, not only general respondents. Convenience sampling is acceptable for public survey distribution through university networks, community groups, online channels, professional contacts, and relevant public networks. The study should intentionally oversample prior victims so that revictimization can be analysed. The exact subgroup target will depend on feasibility, but the final report must state the achieved number of prior victims and repeat victims before interpreting revictimization findings.

For structural model testing, the target total sample size is approximately 200 to 400 respondents, subject to access and feasibility. The final minimum sample size should be checked using power analysis or relevant PLS-SEM sample-size guidance before data collection (Faul et al., 2007; Faul et al., 2009; Hair et al., 2022). If the number of repeat victims is too small for separate modelling, repeat-victim analysis will be treated as descriptive or exploratory.

3.2.3 Conceptual Framework

The conceptual framework integrates TPB, PMT, Cognitive Appraisal Theory, and the seven-factor taxonomy. TPB contributes attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioural control, and protective intention (Ajzen, 1991). PMT contributes perceived severity, perceived vulnerability, response efficacy, self-efficacy, and response cost (Rogers, 1975; Rogers, 1983). Cognitive Appraisal Theory contributes primary appraisal, secondary appraisal, coping response, and reappraisal (Lazarus and Folkman, 1984; Folkman et al., 1986).

In the framework, scam exposure activates cognitive appraisal. Respondents evaluate whether the message, call, offer, relationship, application, or payment request appears legitimate, suspicious, threatening, urgent, beneficial, or harmless. Threat appraisal and coping appraisal influence protection motivation. TPB constructs influence whether protective behaviour is considered useful, socially supported, and personally manageable. The outcome is measured through protective intention, protective behaviour, scenario-based response, reporting capability, and revictimization tendency.

3.2.4 Variables and Constructs

The independent variables are the seven revictimization factor categories and their related TPB, PMT, and appraisal constructs. The mediating variables are protective intention, coping appraisal, and cognitive reappraisal. The dependent variables are revictimization tendency, risky compliance, safe scenario response, reporting capability, and recovery-scam resistance.

3.2.5 Instrument Design

The research instrument will be a structured questionnaire with scenario-based tasks. Items will mainly use a five-point Likert-type scale from strongly disagree to strongly agree, because Likert-type scales are commonly used to measure attitudes, perceptions, intentions, and latent constructs (Likert, 1932). Scenario items will use response options that can be scored as safe, partially safe, or risky.

Table 3.4: Questionnaire Structure

| Section | Content | Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A | Demographic profile | Describe respondent background |
| B | Digital platform and payment exposure | Identify exposure through banking, e-commerce, e-wallets, social media, apps, and messaging |
| C | Cyber fraud exposure and victimization history | Classify prior victims, repeat victims, attempted-scam targets, and no-known-exposure respondents |
| D | Seven revictimization factor items | Measure F1 to F7 directly |
| E | TPB constructs | Measure attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioural control, and protective intention |
| F | PMT constructs | Measure severity, vulnerability, response efficacy, self-efficacy, and response cost |
| G | Cognitive appraisal constructs | Measure primary appraisal, secondary appraisal, coping response, and reappraisal |
| H | Scenario-based scam response | Test realistic decision-making under local scam cues |
| I | Tool-use capability | Test ability to choose NSRC, bank hotline, Semak Mule, Cyber999, SC checks, PDRM, or platform reporting |
| J | Intervention evaluation | Measure module usefulness and pre/post change |

Prior-victim items will ask whether the respondent has experienced a scam before, whether there was financial loss or data compromise, whether the incident was reported, how quickly the respondent acted, whether the respondent was contacted again, whether a recovery service approached them, and whether shame or embarrassment affected help-seeking. The questionnaire will not request account numbers, passwords, banking credentials, OTP/TAC codes, identity-card numbers, raw screenshots, or personal scam evidence that can identify the respondent.

3.2.6 Scenario Design and Scoring

Scenario-based assessment is necessary because self-reported awareness may not reflect behaviour under pressure. Each scenario will include a short local scam prompt, manipulation cues, a decision point, response options, and scoring. The scenarios will use Malaysian scam cues documented in CyberSecurity Malaysia, SC, PDRM-linked, NSRC, and threat-intelligence sources.

Table 3.5: Scenario Coverage and Scoring Logic

| Scenario | Main factor tested | Safe response | Partially safe response | Risky compliance |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fake PDRM/LHDN/BNM/MCMC call | F3, F4, F7 | End call and verify independently through official channel | Ask family/friend before acting but stays engaged | Follow instructions, share data, or transfer money |
| Fake bank/e-wallet/delivery smishing link | F1, F4, F7 | Do not click; access official app/site directly | Delay action but does not verify | Click link, enter details, or pay |
| Traffic summons or government-aid phishing | F1, F3, F4 | Verify through official website/app | Search online but may click supplied link | Pay through supplied link or submit data |
| Malicious APK delivery or wedding/service request | F1, F2, F7 | Refuse APK; use official app store/source | Ask sender but still considers install | Install APK or allow permissions |
| Telegram/social media investment offer | F2, F4, F6, F7 | Check SC Investment Checker/Investor Alert List and Semak Mule | Ask group/admin for proof | Deposit into personal/unrelated account |
| Job-task or loan fee scam | F4, F6, F7 | Reject upfront fee and verify entity | Continue conversation but delays payment | Pay task/unlock/processing fee |
| Love/parcel scam | F5, F6 | Refuse transfer, consult trusted person, verify identity | Delay payment but keeps secrecy | Pay parcel/emergency/customs fee |
| Recovery scam after prior loss | F3, F5, F7 | Refuse fee, contact official channels, preserve evidence | Ask for proof but keeps communicating | Pay recovery fee or contact fake support |

Responses will be scored as follows: safe response = 2, partially safe response = 1, risky compliance = 0. The total scenario score will indicate practical scam-response capability. Separate sub-scores will be created for authority/urgency, social proof, financial opportunity, emotional grooming, malicious APK, and recovery-scam resistance.

3.2.7 Tool-Use Capability Measurement

The study will measure whether respondents can identify the correct protective channel for different scam situations. Tool awareness alone is insufficient; respondents must know which tool to use and when. The tool-use capability score will include:

(a) NSRC 997 and bank hotline for active or recent scam transfers.

(b) Semak Mule for suspicious bank accounts, phone numbers, and company names.

(c) Cyber999/MyCERT for computer security incidents, malicious links, malicious APKs, and cyber incident reporting.

(d) SC Investment Checker and Investor Alert List for investment-related offers.

(e) PDRM reporting for crime reports and investigation records.

(f) Platform reporting for fraudulent social media accounts, marketplace listings, or messaging accounts.

Each tool-use item will present a short situation and ask the respondent to choose the most appropriate response channel. Correct, partially correct, and incorrect choices will be scored separately from confidence.

3.2.8 Data Collection Procedure

Data collection will begin with expert review and pilot testing. Expert review will assess whether the instrument is clear, ethically safe, Malaysia-specific, and aligned with the seven-factor taxonomy. Pilot testing will identify confusing wording, excessive length, unclear scenario choices, and reliability problems.

After refinement, the main questionnaire will be distributed online. Respondents will receive an information statement explaining the study purpose, voluntary participation, confidentiality, right to withdraw, and the sensitive nature of cyber fraud victimization questions. The survey will not request sensitive banking data, passwords, account numbers, OTP/TAC codes, or identifiable scam evidence.

For the intervention evaluation, participants will complete a pre-test, complete the intervention module activities, and then complete a post-test. The pre-test and post-test will measure comparable constructs and scenario outcomes. A delayed follow-up after two to four weeks is recommended if feasible.

3.2.9 Ethical Safeguards

Cyber fraud victimization can involve shame, embarrassment, financial stress, and emotional distress. The study will therefore apply the following safeguards:

(a) Participation is voluntary, and respondents may withdraw without penalty.

(b) The survey will avoid unnecessary personally identifiable information.

(c) No banking credentials, passwords, OTP/TAC codes, account numbers, or raw identifiable evidence will be collected.

(d) Respondents will not be asked to upload scam screenshots, bank statements, identity documents, or chat logs.

(e) Prior-victim questions will be phrased respectfully and will not blame victims.

(f) Reporting and support information will be provided, including bank hotlines, NSRC 997, Semak Mule, Cyber999/MyCERT, SC investor-checking resources, and PDRM reporting channels where relevant.

(g) Data will be stored securely and reported only in aggregate form.

3.2.10 Data Analysis Method

Data analysis will begin with screening for incomplete, duplicate, careless, or inconsistent responses. Descriptive statistics will summarize demographics, digital exposure, victimization history, repeat victimization, reporting behaviour, and scenario performance.

Reliability will be assessed using Cronbach's alpha and composite reliability where appropriate. Nunnally (1978) is commonly used for interpreting internal consistency in early-stage research. Convergent validity may be assessed using Average Variance Extracted, while discriminant validity may be assessed using the Fornell-Larcker criterion and the HTMT ratio (Fornell and Larcker, 1981; Henseler et al., 2015).

Relationship testing will use regression analysis or Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modelling, depending on the final sample size, model complexity, and data quality. PLS-SEM is suitable for prediction-oriented models involving multiple latent constructs and behavioural outcomes (Hair et al., 2022). Path coefficients, significance values, R-squared values, effect sizes, and predictive relevance will be examined where applicable (Cohen, 1988; Hair et al., 2022).

Subgroup analysis will compare prior victims, repeat victims, attempted-scam exposed non-victims, and no-known-exposure respondents. If the repeat-victim subgroup is too small for inferential analysis, findings will be presented descriptively with clear limitations.

Intervention evaluation will compare pre-test and post-test scores for scam detection, self-efficacy, response efficacy, protective intention, tool-use capability, reporting intention, safe scenario response, and recovery-scam resistance. Paired-sample t-tests or non-parametric alternatives will be selected based on data distribution. Pre-test/post-test evaluation is appropriate for measuring change after an intervention, but delayed follow-up is recommended to assess retention.

3.3 Justification

The selected methodology is appropriate because cyber fraud revictimization is a behavioural and psychosocial problem that cannot be measured through awareness questions alone. A quantitative survey allows the study to measure theoretical constructs and compare respondent groups. Scenario tasks allow the study to test practical response under realistic scam cues. Pre-test/post-test evaluation allows the study to examine whether the intervention modules produce measurable improvement.

The integration of TPB, PMT, and Cognitive Appraisal Theory is justified because each theory explains a different layer of the problem. TPB explains protective intention and perceived control. PMT explains threat and coping appraisal. Cognitive Appraisal Theory explains interpretation and coping under pressure. The seven-factor taxonomy then converts these theories into Malaysia-specific measurement categories.

The methodology also responds to the weakness identified in the audit: revictimization must be measured, not assumed. By separating prior victims, repeat victims, attempted-scam targets, and no-known-exposure respondents, the study can examine whether the proposed factors operate differently across groups.

3.4 Performance Measurement

Performance measurement in this study refers to the quality of the instrument, the strength of the conceptual model, and the effectiveness of the intervention modules. The key measures are shown in Table 3.6.

3.5 Chapter Summary

This chapter presented the methodology for studying cyber fraud revictimization among the public in Malaysia. The methodology uses a quantitative survey, scenario-based assessment, respondent subgroup classification, tool-use capability scoring, and pre-test/post-test intervention evaluation. It operationalises revictimization through prior victimization, repeat victimization, repeated exposure, risky compliance, delayed reporting, shame/help-seeking barriers, and recovery-scam vulnerability.

The chapter also defined the sampling strategy, constructs, questionnaire sections, scenario scoring, ethical safeguards, data analysis methods, and performance measurement criteria. The next chapter presents the research design and implementation of the intervention modules.
