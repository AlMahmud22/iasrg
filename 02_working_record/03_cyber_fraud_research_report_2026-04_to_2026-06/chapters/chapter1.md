Study on Identification of Factors and Development of Modules for Methods to Prevent Cyber Fraud Revictimization Problems Among Public in Malaysia

CHAPTER 1 INTRODUCTION

1.1 Introduction

Cyber fraud has become a serious digital safety problem in Malaysia because everyday financial, social, commercial, and public-service activities now depend heavily on online banking, e-commerce, e-wallets, mobile applications, social media, and messaging platforms. These services create convenience, but they also create repeated contact points through which fraudsters can deceive the public. Malaysian users may encounter phishing links, smishing messages, fake government-aid pages, malicious Android Package Kit (APK) files, fake investment schemes, job-task scams, e-commerce fraud, love scams, and impersonation calls using the names of agencies such as PDRM, BNM, LHDN, MCMC, SC, NSRC, banks, courts, and delivery companies.

Cyber fraud in this research refers to deception conducted through digital or communication technologies to obtain money, personal information, credentials, access, or other unlawful advantage. The term includes both technical and social-engineering forms of fraud. Technical compromise may occur through malicious APKs, phishing pages, account takeover, or credential theft, but many cyber fraud cases begin with persuasion. Victims may be manipulated into clicking a link, installing an application, transferring money, sharing an OTP/TAC, trusting a fake authority, or continuing communication with a fraudulent person. Therefore, this study treats cyber fraud as both a cybersecurity problem and a behavioural decision-making problem.

The focus of this research is cyber fraud revictimization among the public in Malaysia. A prior victim is an individual who has previously experienced confirmed cyber fraud victimization, such as financial loss, credential compromise, unauthorised use of personal information, or a reported scam incident. Repeat victimization refers to a later scam incident after an earlier victimization experience. Revictimization tendency refers to measurable vulnerability after prior scam exposure, including repeated risky compliance, repeated scam exposure with unsafe responses, susceptibility to recovery scams, delayed reporting caused by shame or confusion, and low confidence in using protective channels. The intervention target is therefore not only general awareness but also the decision process that determines whether a prior victim or exposed user can pause, verify, refuse, report, and recover safely.

This study is grounded in three behavioural theories: the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB), Protection Motivation Theory (PMT), and Cognitive Appraisal Theory. TPB explains how attitude, subjective norms, and perceived behavioural control influence protective intention (Ajzen, 1991). PMT explains how perceived severity, perceived vulnerability, response efficacy, self-efficacy, and response cost influence motivation to protect oneself (Rogers, 1975; Rogers, 1983). Cognitive Appraisal Theory explains how people interpret stressful or uncertain situations and select coping responses (Lazarus and Folkman, 1984; Folkman et al., 1986). These theories are used to identify factors that contribute to cyber fraud revictimization and to guide the development of intervention modules.

1.2 Problem Background

Malaysia has developed multiple institutional responses to cyber fraud. These include the National Scam Response Centre (NSRC), Semak Mule, Cyber999/MyCERT reporting, Bank Negara Malaysia anti-fraud measures, the National Fraud Portal, Securities Commission Malaysia investor-protection tools, MCMC content-blocking cooperation, and public scam-awareness campaigns. Bank Negara Malaysia reported that the National Fraud Portal was launched in September 2024 and supported faster identification of suspected fraud transactions and mule accounts (Bank Negara Malaysia, 2025). The Ministry of Finance Malaysia reported that NSRC had received 122,603 calls from its launch on 12 October 2022 to August 2024 and that suspicious transactions had been blocked through coordinated response activities (Ministry of Finance Malaysia, 2024). CyberSecurity Malaysia reported that fraud was the dominant Cyber999 incident category in Q4 2024 and Q1 2025 (CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025a; CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025b).

These institutional tools are important, but they do not remove the behavioural gap. A user who is frightened by a fake authority call may still transfer money. A user who sees a limited-time investment opportunity may skip verification. A prior victim may avoid reporting because of shame or may trust a fake recovery service that claims it can retrieve lost funds. A digitally literate user may still click a convincing link if overconfidence makes verification feel unnecessary. Therefore, the central problem is not only whether the public has heard of scams, but whether people can act safely when fraudsters use urgency, authority, social proof, emotional grooming, and financial pressure.

The current Malaysian evidence supports this behavioural focus. Balakrishnan, Ahhmed and Basheer (2025) found that overconfidence and social influence significantly predicted online fraud victimization among Malaysian adults, which shows that awareness alone is not enough. Securities Commission Malaysia has warned that investment scams use high-return promises, limited-time framing, fake testimonials, social media promotion, celebrity or personality misuse, clone entities, and deposits into personal or unrelated accounts (Securities Commission Malaysia, n.d.). CyberSecurity Malaysia has documented local phishing, smishing, vishing, malicious APK, impersonation, government-aid, delivery, traffic-summons, and bank/e-wallet themes (CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025a; CyberSecurity Malaysia, 2025b). These patterns show that Malaysian scam cues are local, familiar, and psychologically targeted.

However, direct research on cyber fraud revictimization in Malaysia remains limited. Existing evidence strongly supports the study of fraud victimization, scam infrastructure, reporting systems, and behavioural susceptibility, but it does not fully explain how prior victims become vulnerable again. This creates a research gap. The study must measure revictimization directly instead of assuming it from general scam prevalence. It must distinguish prior victims, repeat victims, attempted-scam targets, and respondents with no known exposure. It must also examine whether the public can use protective tools under realistic scam pressure, not merely whether they know the tools exist.

This research responds to that gap by using a seven-factor taxonomy of cyber fraud revictimization. The seven factors are: overconfidence and awareness-behaviour gap; social influence, trust transfer, and social proof; authority impersonation and institutional compliance; urgency, fear, and high-arousal compliance; emotional grooming, shame, and post-victim recovery vulnerability; financial need, opportunity pressure, and reward/FOMO bias; and protective self-efficacy, verification friction, and recovery/reporting capability. These factors connect Malaysian scam evidence with TPB, PMT, and Cognitive Appraisal Theory, and they provide the backbone for the questionnaire, scenario tasks, intervention modules, and evaluation design.

1.3 Research Aim

The aim of this research is to identify psychosocial factors that contribute to cyber fraud revictimization among the public in Malaysia and to develop and evaluate theory-based intervention modules that strengthen protective behaviour, verification capability, reporting confidence, and resistance to repeated victimization.

1.4 Research Question

The research questions of this study are:

(a) What psychosocial factors contribute to cyber fraud revictimization tendency among the public in Malaysia, based on the Theory of Planned Behavior, Protection Motivation Theory, and Cognitive Appraisal Theory?

(b) How do the seven revictimization factor categories relate to protective intention, protective behaviour, scenario-based risky compliance, reporting behaviour, and recovery-scam resistance?

(c) How can theory-based intervention modules be designed to address Malaysian scam cues, prior-victim vulnerability, verification friction, shame, and repeated targeting?

(d) How effective are the proposed intervention modules in improving scam cue detection, protective self-efficacy, response efficacy, safe scenario response, reporting intention, and revictimization resistance?

1.5 Research Objectives

The objectives of this research are:

(a) To identify and operationalise the psychosocial factors that contribute to cyber fraud revictimization among the public in Malaysia using the Theory of Planned Behavior, Protection Motivation Theory, Cognitive Appraisal Theory, and the seven-factor revictimization taxonomy.

(b) To develop theory-based intervention modules that address overconfidence, social influence, authority impersonation, urgency, emotional grooming, financial pressure, verification friction, reporting capability, and post-victim recovery vulnerability.

(c) To evaluate the effectiveness of the intervention modules using pre-test and post-test measures of scam detection, protective intention, self-efficacy, response efficacy, scenario-based safe response, reporting capability, and recovery-scam resistance.

1.6 Research Scope

The scopes of the research are:

(a) This study focuses on cyber fraud affecting the public in Malaysia, particularly scams involving social engineering, impersonation, phishing, smishing, malicious APKs, investment schemes, e-commerce fraud, job scams, love scams, recovery scams, non-existent loan scams, and mule-account-related payment deception.

(b) The target respondents include Malaysian internet users who use digital communication, social media, e-commerce, mobile banking, e-wallets, or related online services. The study will distinguish prior victims, repeat victims, attempted-scam exposed non-victims, and respondents with no known scam exposure.

(c) The study focuses on behavioural and psychosocial factors rather than malware reverse engineering, forensic investigation, syndicate profiling, or technical banking-system design.

(d) The theoretical scope is limited to TPB, PMT, and Cognitive Appraisal Theory, supported by the seven-factor taxonomy developed for this research.

(e) The intervention scope covers awareness, cue detection, cognitive reappraisal, threat and coping appraisal, verification and reporting routines, shame reduction, trusted social support, and revictimization resistance.

(f) The study will use official, regulatory, and academic sources as the main evidence base. News and vendor threat-intelligence sources will only be used as secondary support or technical case context, with caveats.

1.7 Research Contribution

This research contributes academically by developing a Malaysia-specific behavioural framework for cyber fraud revictimization. Instead of treating cyber fraud only as a technical security issue or a general awareness problem, the study connects revictimization to intention, social influence, threat appraisal, coping appraisal, real-time cognitive interpretation, emotional recovery, and practical verification capability.

This research contributes methodologically by operationalising revictimization as measurable behaviour and history, not only as a general concept. The study separates prior victimization, repeat victimization, attempted-scam exposure, scenario-based risky compliance, delayed reporting, recovery-scam vulnerability, and tool-use capability. This distinction is necessary because a person may be aware of scams but still fail to perform safe actions under pressure.

This research contributes practically by developing intervention modules that can be adapted for public education, universities, community programmes, banks, regulators, cybersecurity awareness teams, and victim-support initiatives. The modules are designed around Malaysian scam cues and local protective tools such as NSRC 997, bank hotlines, Semak Mule, Cyber999/MyCERT, PDRM reporting, and Securities Commission Malaysia investor-checking resources.
