CHAPTER 4 RESEARCH DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION

4.1 Introduction

This chapter presents the research design and implementation plan for the cyber fraud revictimization prevention modules. The chapter translates the seven-factor taxonomy, Malaysian scam evidence, and behavioural theories into a practical intervention framework. The purpose is not to create a general awareness campaign only, but to design structured modules that can be evaluated through measurable pre-test and post-test outcomes.

The intervention is designed for Malaysian internet users, especially individuals with prior cyber fraud exposure or victimization. The modules are also relevant to public awareness programmes, university digital-safety programmes, community education, financial-literacy activities, banking awareness, and regulator-led scam-prevention initiatives. The design uses TPB to strengthen protective intention and perceived behavioural control, PMT to strengthen threat and coping appraisal, and Cognitive Appraisal Theory to train cue detection and reappraisal under pressure.

4.2 Proposed Solution

The proposed solution is a five-module behavioural intervention for cyber fraud revictimization prevention. The modules address the seven revictimization factors identified in Chapter 2. Each module includes learning content, Malaysian scam cues, scenario activities, tool-use practice, and measurable outcomes. The modules are intended to be delivered sequentially because each module builds toward practical protective behaviour.

The intervention is built around the protective routine: Pause, Verify, Refuse, Report, and Recover. Pause means stopping before clicking, transferring, installing, sharing, or responding. Verify means checking through independently sourced official channels. Refuse means rejecting secrecy, urgent payment, safe-account transfer, APK installation, credential sharing, OTP/TAC sharing, and recovery-fee requests. Report means contacting the correct channel, such as bank hotline, NSRC 997, PDRM, Cyber999/MyCERT, SC resources, or platform reporting. Recover means preserving evidence, protecting accounts, warning contacts, seeking support, and avoiding recovery scams.

4.2.1 Design Principles

The modules follow five design principles. First, awareness must be separated from behaviour because self-rated knowledge does not always predict safe action. Second, every module must use Malaysia-specific cues, such as PDRM, LHDN, MCMC, BNM, NSRC, banks, e-wallets, delivery services, government aid, traffic summons, SC-licensed entities, Semak Mule, and Cyber999. Third, fear-based content must be paired with coping steps so that the intervention does not increase panic or avoidance. Fourth, prior victims must be treated respectfully, without blame. Fifth, every module must be evaluated through measurable outcomes.

4.3 Experiment Design

The intervention evaluation uses a pre-test/post-test design. Participants complete a pre-test measuring scam cue detection, protective intention, self-efficacy, response efficacy, tool-use capability, reporting intention, scenario response, and recovery-scam resistance. Participants then complete the five intervention modules. After the modules, they complete a post-test using comparable items and scenarios. If feasible, a delayed follow-up test is conducted two to four weeks later.

The evaluation will compare perception-based outcomes and performance-based outcomes. Perception-based outcomes include protective intention, perceived behavioural control, response efficacy, self-efficacy, reporting intention, and help-seeking confidence. Performance-based outcomes include scenario response score, correct tool selection, correct reporting path, and recovery-scam resistance. This separation is important because a participant may feel confident but still choose an unsafe response in a scenario.

Table 4.2: Intervention Evaluation Design

| Stage | Activity | Measurement |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pre-test | Baseline questionnaire and scenarios | Seven factors, theory constructs, tool-use capability, scenario score |
| Module delivery | Five sequential modules | Completion of learning activities and scenario practice |
| Post-test | Comparable questionnaire and scenarios | Change in intention, self-efficacy, response efficacy, tool use, scenario performance |
| Optional follow-up | Delayed test after two to four weeks | Retention of safe responses and reporting capability |
| Analysis | Compare pre/post and subgroup results | Intervention effect and prior-victim relevance |

4.4 Parameter and Testing Method

The main testing parameters are:

(a) Scam cue detection score: number of correctly identified scam cues across authority, urgency, social proof, emotional grooming, financial opportunity, malicious APK, phishing, and recovery-scam scenarios.

(b) Scenario safe-response score: score based on safe response = 2, partially safe response = 1, and risky compliance = 0.

(c) Protective intention score: intention to pause, verify, refuse, report, seek support, and recover safely.

(d) Self-efficacy score: confidence in performing protective actions without relying on the scammer's instructions.

(e) Response efficacy score: belief that verification, reporting, evidence preservation, and official tools can reduce harm.

(f) Tool-use capability score: ability to choose the correct Malaysian channel for a given scam type.

(g) Reporting speed/intention score: likelihood of contacting bank hotline, NSRC 997, PDRM, Cyber999/MyCERT, SC, or platform channels quickly when needed.

(h) Recovery-scam resistance score: ability to reject fake recovery agents, fake NSRC accounts, fake lawyers, and fee-based recovery claims.

(i) Shame/help-seeking score: willingness to disclose scam exposure, seek trusted support, and report without self-blame.

4.5 Module 1: Cyber Fraud and Revictimization Awareness

Module 1 introduces cyber fraud and revictimization in the Malaysian context. The module explains that scams are not always caused by technical hacking. Many scams begin when victims are persuaded to authorise a transfer, click a link, install an APK, trust an online relationship, follow a fake authority, or believe a high-return offer.

The module covers common Malaysian scam types, including authority impersonation, phishing, smishing, traffic summons scams, government-aid scams, delivery and e-wallet scams, investment and clone-firm scams, job-task scams, love and parcel scams, malicious APK scams, mule-account payment deception, and recovery scams. The module also explains that prior victimization does not automatically make a person safer. Prior victims may experience shame, low confidence, repeated contact, or hope for recovery, which can increase vulnerability to new scams.

Table 4.4: Module 1 Content Plan

| Component | Description | Evidence basis | Learning output |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Malaysian scam overview | Introduce local scam types and channels | CyberSecurity Malaysia (2025a, 2025b); Securities Commission Malaysia (n.d.) | Participants identify common Malaysian scam types |
| Behavioural manipulation | Explain fear, urgency, authority, social proof, reward, and emotional grooming | Balakrishnan et al. (2025); Alavi et al. (2018) | Participants understand why awareness alone is insufficient |
| Revictimization definition | Explain prior victim, repeat victimization, revictimization tendency, and recovery scams | Bernama (2025a); Securities Commission Malaysia (2022) | Participants recognize repeated vulnerability after prior exposure |
| Local response tools | Introduce NSRC, bank hotline, Semak Mule, Cyber999, PDRM, and SC resources | Bank Negara Malaysia (2025); Ministry of Finance Malaysia (2024); Royal Malaysia Police (2025) | Participants know the main protective channels |

4.6 Module 2: Scam Cue Detection and Cognitive Reappraisal

Module 2 trains participants to identify scam cues before accepting the scammer's framing. Cognitive Appraisal Theory guides this module because scam situations depend on how a person interprets the event. A fake police call may be appraised as a real legal threat. A Telegram investment group may be appraised as a legitimate opportunity. A romantic contact may be appraised as trustworthy. A malicious APK may be appraised as a normal delivery or service tool.

The module teaches participants to reappraise suspicious situations. Urgency should be reappraised as a warning cue. Authority claims should be reappraised as requiring independent verification. Secrecy should be reappraised as isolation. Guaranteed return should be reappraised as investment risk. APK installation requests should be reappraised as technical compromise risk. Recovery-fee requests should be reappraised as repeated targeting.

Table 4.5: Scam Cue Detection Matrix

| Scam cue | Common Malaysian example | Trigger | Safer reappraisal |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Authority claim | PDRM, BNM, LHDN, MCMC, SC, NSRC, bank, court | Obedience and fear | Official claims must be verified through independent channels |
| Urgency | Account frozen, arrest, tax case, payment due today | Panic and time pressure | Urgency itself is a scam warning sign |
| Secrecy | Do not tell family, bank, police, or friends | Isolation | Legitimate agencies do not require secrecy from support networks |
| Safe account | Transfer money to protect it | Fear of loss | Safe-account transfer requests are unsafe |
| Social proof | Testimonials, Telegram profit screenshots, celebrity image | Trust transfer | Group approval and images can be fabricated |
| Guaranteed return | High profit with no risk | Reward and FOMO | Guaranteed high returns are investment scam cues |
| APK request | Install delivery, wedding, service, or statement APK | Familiarity and technical confusion | APKs from messages or unknown sources should be refused |
| Emotional grooming | Online partner asks for money or parcel fee | Attachment and obligation | Emotional trust does not replace verification |
| Recovery promise | Pay a fee to recover lost scam money | Hope after loss | Recovery services can be scams; use official channels |

4.7 Module 3: Threat and Coping Appraisal Strengthening

Module 3 strengthens protection motivation by balancing threat awareness and coping confidence. PMT is central to this module. Participants should understand that cyber fraud can cause serious financial, privacy, emotional, and social consequences, but they should also believe that protective actions are practical and effective.

Threat information must be handled carefully. If participants only learn that scams are dangerous, they may become anxious or avoid reporting. Therefore, every threat message is paired with coping actions. For example, after learning about fake investment schemes, participants practise checking SC Investment Checker, Investor Alert List, and Semak Mule. After learning about active scam transfers, participants practise choosing bank hotline and NSRC 997 as early reporting channels. After learning about malicious APKs, participants practise refusing APK installation and using official app sources.

Table 4.6: PMT-Based Module Design

| PMT construct | Module strategy | Example activity | Expected effect |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Perceived severity | Explain financial loss, privacy exposure, emotional harm, and repeated targeting | Case-based discussion using Malaysian scam examples | Participants recognize seriousness |
| Perceived vulnerability | Explain that students, professionals, older adults, online shoppers, investors, and prior victims can all be targeted | Personal exposure reflection | Participants recognize personal relevance |
| Response efficacy | Demonstrate that reporting and verification can reduce harm | Match scam type to bank hotline, NSRC, Semak Mule, Cyber999, SC, or PDRM | Participants believe protective actions work |
| Self-efficacy | Practise refusing, verifying, and reporting | Role-play fake authority call and investment offer | Participants feel capable of acting safely |
| Response cost | Reduce embarrassment, confusion, time pressure, and fear of missing out | Provide scripts and checklists | Participants perceive safe action as easier |

4.8 Module 4: Verification, Reporting, and Protective Action Routine

Module 4 converts awareness and motivation into practical action. This is the most operational module because it teaches participants exactly what to do under pressure. The routine is Pause, Verify, Refuse, Report, and Recover.

Pause is trained first because urgency is a common scam mechanism. Verify is trained through independent channels, not through phone numbers, links, QR codes, or social media accounts supplied by the suspicious sender. Refuse is trained through short scripts that reject unsafe instructions. Report is trained by matching the scam type to the appropriate channel. Recover is trained through account protection, evidence preservation, support-seeking, and recovery-scam avoidance.

Table 4.7: Protective Action Routine

| Step | Behaviour | Practical instruction | Tool/channel examples |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Pause | Stop before acting | Do not click, transfer, install, disclose, or continue under pressure | Personal pause rule |
| Verify | Check independently | Use official websites, apps, directories, or trusted published contacts | Semak Mule, SC Investment Checker, bank official site, official agency site |
| Refuse | Reject unsafe instructions | Refuse secrecy, safe account, OTP/TAC sharing, APK installation, and fee requests | Refusal scripts |
| Report | Notify correct channel | Contact the channel suitable to the scam type | Bank hotline, NSRC 997, PDRM, Cyber999/MyCERT, platform report |
| Recover | Reduce further harm | Preserve evidence, change passwords, warn contacts, monitor accounts, avoid recovery scams | Bank support, PDRM report, Cyber999, trusted support |

Table 4.8: Tool-Use Capability Guide

| Situation | Preferred response channel | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Active or recent transfer to suspected scammer | Bank hotline and NSRC 997 | Speed matters for blocking or tracing funds |
| Suspicious bank account, phone number, or company name | Semak Mule | Supports pre-transfer checking |
| Suspicious investment entity or high-return scheme | SC Investment Checker and Investor Alert List; Semak Mule for account checking | Investment legitimacy and payment account checking |
| Malicious APK, phishing link, cyber incident, or compromised account | Cyber999/MyCERT and relevant platform/security support | Computer security incident response |
| Confirmed fraud requiring crime record or investigation | PDRM report | Formal reporting and investigation record |
| Fraudulent social media profile, marketplace listing, or messaging account | Platform reporting plus official reporting where loss occurs | Reduces exposure and supports evidence trail |

4.9 Module 5: Recovery, Shame Reduction, Social Support, and Revictimization Resistance

Module 5 focuses specifically on prior victims and people at risk of revictimization. This module is necessary because prior victimization can create emotional and behavioural consequences that general awareness modules do not address. Victims may feel ashamed, blame themselves, avoid reporting, distrust official channels, or become desperate to recover lost money.

The module reframes victimization as manipulation rather than personal failure. This does not remove individual responsibility for safe action, but it reduces victim-blaming and encourages help-seeking. The module also teaches that recovery infrastructure can be impersonated. Bernama reported a PDRM warning about fake Facebook accounts using the NSRC name and image, and PDRM stated that NSRC receives calls through 997 and does not contact individuals by phone or WhatsApp (Bernama, 2025a). This makes fake recovery and fake support channels a necessary part of revictimization training.

| Component | Purpose | Activity | Outcome |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Shame reduction | Reduce avoidance and self-blame | Reframe scam as structured manipulation | Higher help-seeking intention |
| Trusted-contact routine | Create safe social support | Identify one or two trusted verification contacts | Better protective social support |
| Reporting confidence | Encourage timely reporting | Practise choosing bank hotline, NSRC, PDRM, Cyber999, or SC channel | Faster reporting intention |
| Evidence preservation | Support recovery and investigation | Learn what to save without uploading sensitive data to the survey | Better recovery readiness |

4.10 Scenario-Based Intervention Activities

Scenario-based activities are used in every module because cyber fraud response is situational. Participants must practise recognizing scam cues, choosing safe responses, and selecting the correct reporting or verification channel. Table 4.10 shows the proposed activities.

Table 4.10: Proposed Scenario-Based Activities

| Scenario | Scam type | Main cue tested | Safe response |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Caller claims to be from PDRM and says the respondent is linked to money laundering | Authority impersonation | Authority, fear, urgency | End call and verify independently; do not transfer money |
| SMS says bank/e-wallet account will be frozen unless a link is clicked | Phishing/smishing | Urgency and fake identity | Do not click; use official app or website |
| Message offers government aid or traffic summons settlement through a link | Government-aid/traffic phishing | Familiar local institution | Verify through official government channel |
| WhatsApp seller asks respondent to install APK for delivery tracking | Malicious APK | Routine service request | Refuse APK and use official delivery/app-store source |
| Telegram group shows guaranteed investment profit and testimonials | Investment scam | Social proof and FOMO | Check SC resources and avoid deposits to unrelated accounts |
| Job platform asks for task fee or unlock fee | Job/task scam | Financial opportunity pressure | Refuse upfront fee and verify legitimacy |
| Online romantic partner asks for parcel or emergency payment | Love/parcel scam | Emotional grooming and obligation | Do not transfer; consult trusted person and verify |
| Person claims to recover previous scam loss for a fee | Recovery scam | Hope after loss | Refuse fee; report through official channels |

4.11 Expected Implementation Outputs

The implementation should produce the following outputs:

(a) A five-module intervention package.

(b) A pre-test and post-test instrument.

(c) A scenario bank covering Malaysian authority, phishing, smishing, investment, job, love, malicious APK, e-commerce/payment, and recovery-scam cues.

(d) A tool-use capability section covering NSRC, bank hotline, Semak Mule, Cyber999/MyCERT, SC resources, PDRM, and platform reporting.

(e) A scoring guide for scenario safe response, tool-use capability, recovery-scam resistance, and intervention improvement.

4.12 Chapter Summary

This chapter presented the research design and implementation plan for the cyber fraud revictimization prevention modules. The proposed solution consists of five modules: cyber fraud and revictimization awareness; scam cue detection and cognitive reappraisal; threat and coping appraisal strengthening; verification, reporting, and protective action routines; and recovery, shame reduction, social support, and revictimization resistance.

The chapter also explained the pre-test/post-test experiment design, testing parameters, module content, Malaysian scenario activities, tool-use capability guide, and expected implementation outputs. The next chapter presents the results, analysis, and discussion structure for the study.
