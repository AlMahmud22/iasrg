CHAPTER 6 CONCLUSION

6.1 Introduction

This chapter concludes the research on identifying factors and developing intervention modules to prevent cyber fraud revictimization among the public in Malaysia. The chapter summarizes the study's contribution, explains how the research objectives are addressed, and presents suggestions for improvement and future work. No new results are introduced in this chapter.

6.2 Achievement of Project Objectives

The first objective was to identify and operationalise the psychosocial factors that contribute to cyber fraud revictimization among the public in Malaysia. This objective is addressed through the integration of TPB, PMT, Cognitive Appraisal Theory, Malaysian cyber fraud evidence, and the seven-factor revictimization taxonomy. The seven factors are overconfidence and awareness-behaviour gap; social influence, trust transfer, and social proof; authority impersonation and institutional compliance; urgency, fear, and high-arousal compliance; emotional grooming, shame, and post-victim recovery vulnerability; financial need, opportunity pressure, and reward/FOMO bias; and protective self-efficacy, verification friction, and recovery/reporting capability. These factors provide a measurable structure for questionnaire items, scenario tasks, tool-use capability scoring, and intervention evaluation.

The second objective was to develop theory-based intervention modules that address cyber fraud revictimization risk. This objective is addressed through five modules: cyber fraud and revictimization awareness; scam cue detection and cognitive reappraisal; threat and coping appraisal strengthening; verification, reporting, and protective action routines; and recovery, shame reduction, social support, and revictimization resistance. The modules translate theory into practical learning activities and Malaysian scenarios, including authority impersonation, phishing, smishing, investment scams, job scams, love and parcel scams, malicious APKs, e-commerce/payment scams, and recovery scams.

The third objective was to evaluate the effectiveness of the intervention modules. This objective is addressed through the proposed pre-test/post-test evaluation design. The evaluation measures scam cue detection, protective intention, self-efficacy, response efficacy, tool-use capability, reporting intention, scenario-based safe response, recovery-scam resistance, and shame/help-seeking. Actual effectiveness findings must be reported in Chapter 5 after the survey and intervention data are collected and analysed.

This study contributes to cyber fraud prevention by shifting the focus from general awareness to measurable protective behaviour. It recognizes that a person may know about scams but still fail to verify under authority pressure, urgency, emotional grooming, social proof, or financial temptation. It also recognizes that prior victims may need recovery-oriented support, not only warnings aimed at first-time prevention.

6.3 Suggestions for Improvement and Future Works

Several improvements should be considered when completing and extending this research. First, the study should recruit enough prior victims and repeat victims to make revictimization analysis meaningful. If the repeat-victim subgroup is small, the findings should be reported as exploratory rather than conclusive.

Second, future work should include delayed follow-up testing. Immediate post-test improvement may show that participants learned the module content, but it does not prove that safer behaviour is retained over time. A two-to-four-week follow-up would strengthen the evaluation.

Third, future research should consider a comparison group if feasible. A pre-test/post-test design can show improvement after intervention, but a comparison group would provide stronger evidence that the change was caused by the modules rather than repeated testing or outside exposure.

Fourth, the intervention should be tested in different delivery formats. Possible formats include face-to-face workshops, web-based training, mobile micro-learning, chatbot-based scam simulations, and gamified scenario practice. Digital delivery may be especially useful because cyber fraud itself occurs through digital environments, but it must be designed without collecting sensitive victim information.

Fifth, future work should strengthen collaboration with banks, regulators, universities, community organisations, cybersecurity agencies, and victim-support channels. Tools such as NSRC 997, Semak Mule, Cyber999/MyCERT, SC Investment Checker, bank hotlines, PDRM reporting, and platform reporting are valuable only when the public knows when and how to use them.

In conclusion, cyber fraud revictimization among the public in Malaysia is a behavioural, psychological, financial, and institutional challenge. Technical controls and public warnings remain important, but they are not sufficient when fraudsters manipulate trust, fear, urgency, authority, social proof, emotional attachment, and financial hope. This research provides a structured framework for identifying revictimization factors and designing practical intervention modules that strengthen protective decision-making, reporting confidence, and recovery resistance.
