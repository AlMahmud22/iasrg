# Project Analysis: Cyber Fraud Revictimization Research

## 1. Simple Project Understanding

This folder contains the internship research work for a project under Dr. Siti Hajar Othman related to cyber fraud revictimization among the Malaysian public. The main academic direction is not only to describe cyber scams, but to understand why people remain vulnerable after a first scam exposure or victimization event, and how theory-based prevention modules can reduce repeat risk.

The project is built around three theory foundations:

- Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB): attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioural control, intention, and action.
- Protection Motivation Theory (PMT): threat appraisal, coping appraisal, severity, vulnerability, response efficacy, self-efficacy, and response cost.
- Cognitive Appraisal Theory: how people interpret stressful or uncertain scam situations and choose coping responses.

The lecturer's recent task was to research seven main categories of cyber scam revictimization factors:

1. Psychological factors
2. Behavioural factors
3. Socio-demographic factors
4. Technological factors
5. Environmental factors
6. Institutional factors
7. Temporal or time-related factors

The latest submitted report reframed these broad categories into seven practical scam-risk mechanisms:

1. Overconfidence
2. Social influence
3. Authority impersonation
4. Urgency and fear
5. Emotional grooming
6. Financial pressure
7. Protective capability

That reframing is useful because it makes the factors easier to measure and turn into intervention modules. However, for the final research paper, the mapping between the lecturer's original seven categories and the report's seven practical mechanisms must be made explicit.

## 4. Current Main Topic

The topic can be stated simply as:

> Cyber fraud revictimization among the public in Malaysia, focusing on the psychosocial, behavioural, technological, institutional, environmental, socio-demographic, and time-related factors that make people vulnerable to repeated scam exposure or repeated victimization, and developing theory-based prevention modules to reduce that risk.

The key idea is that cyber fraud is not only a technical hacking problem. Many scam cases depend on social engineering, trust, fear, urgency, authority pressure, emotional manipulation, financial temptation, and lack of reporting or verification capability.

The research should therefore focus on behaviour under scam pressure, especially among prior victims or people repeatedly exposed to scam attempts.

## 5. What the Latest Seven-Factor Report Already Does Well

The latest report does several useful things:

- It defines revictimization more broadly than "losing money twice". It includes repeat exposure, risky compliance, repeated contact, fake recovery targeting, delayed reporting, and continued vulnerability.
- It connects scam prevention to behaviour, emotion, social pressure, institutional trust, and practical capability.
- It uses Malaysian examples and public response channels such as NSRC 997, Semak Mule, Cyber999, Securities Commission investor checks, PDRM, banks, and CyberSecurity Malaysia.
- It separates conceptual figures from proof screenshots. This is important because AI-generated figures can explain concepts, but should not be treated as evidence.
- It includes a measurement design with respondent groups, questionnaire areas, scenario tasks, and a safe/partial/risky response scoring method.
- It includes an intervention design with five modules: awareness, cue detection, appraisal, verification/reporting, and recovery support.
- It groups references by source type, which is easier to understand than mixing official reports, academic papers, news, and vendor threat intelligence together.

## 6. Important Gap: Original Categories vs Current Practical Factors

The lecturer gave seven broad factor categories. The latest report uses seven practical mechanisms. These are related, but not exactly the same.

Suggested mapping:

| Lecturer category | Covered in current report? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Psychological factors | Strongly covered | Urgency/fear, emotional grooming, shame, overconfidence, appraisal. |
| Behavioural factors | Strongly covered | Awareness-behaviour gap, risky compliance, verification habits, reporting behaviour. |
| Socio-demographic factors | Weakly covered | Demographics are mentioned in measurement design, but not developed as a full factor. Needs age, gender, income, education, employment, digital literacy, prior-victim status, and vulnerable groups. |
| Technological factors | Partly covered | Malicious APK, phishing, smishing, fake links, platform exposure are included, but mostly as scam examples rather than a full factor category. |
| Environmental factors | Partly covered | Social media groups, WhatsApp/Telegram, marketplace contexts, social proof, community pressure, and economic environment need more structure. |
| Institutional factors | Strongly but indirectly covered | Authority impersonation and reporting channels are covered. Need stronger discussion of trust in institutions, bank response, enforcement limits, NSRC, PDRM, SC, MCMC, BNM, and CyberSecurity Malaysia. |
| Temporal factors | Partly covered | Urgency/fear covers time pressure, but temporal factors should also include delayed reporting, repeated targeting after victimization, seasonal sale scams, recovery-scam timing, and time since first victimization. |

This is probably the biggest structural issue before turning the work into a strong research paper. The final paper should either:

1. Keep the lecturer's seven broad categories as the main framework, and use the practical mechanisms as sub-factors; or
2. Keep the practical seven-factor model, but clearly explain how it operationalizes the lecturer's original seven categories.

The safer academic path is option 1: use the lecturer's seven categories as the main structure, then place the current practical mechanisms inside them.

## 7. What Is Needed Before a Full Research Paper

The current files are strong for a progress report, but not yet a complete academic research paper. The next phase should focus on research validity.

Main needs:

- A sharper operational definition of revictimization.
- A clear conceptual model showing how TPB, PMT, Cognitive Appraisal Theory, and the seven factor categories connect.
- A source-checked Malaysian evidence base.
- A full measurement instrument with actual questionnaire items.
- Scenario tasks written as realistic but fictional Malaysian scam situations.
- A sampling plan that includes prior victims and non-victims.
- Ethical safeguards for sensitive victimization questions.
- A statistical analysis plan.
- A real intervention module design.
- A plan to evaluate the module using pre-test/post-test and possibly delayed follow-up.

## 8. Source and Evidence Issues to Watch

The current files contain many useful references, but they should not be treated as final until checked.

Known concerns from reading the files:

- Older Chapter 2 drafts include some broad web/vendor/news sources that may be useful for context but should not carry the main academic argument.
- Some older references contain warning signs such as "citation needed", generic links, or claims that need direct confirmation.
- Some statistics appear differently across drafts. For example, one version uses "RM19 million" while another older reference text says "RM19 billion". These must be verified before final submission.
- Future/current-year sources such as 2025 and 2026 reports need live URL checking, date checking, and source credibility notes.
- News sources should be used mainly for case examples or public statements, not as the main source for prevalence unless they quote official agencies.
- Vendor threat intelligence such as Kaspersky, Sift, ThreatMark, or similar sources should be used for scam mechanism explanation, not national prevalence claims.

Recommended evidence hierarchy:

1. Official Malaysian sources: BNM, PDRM, CCID, NSRC/MOF, CyberSecurity Malaysia/MyCERT, Securities Commission Malaysia, MCMC, NACSA.
2. Academic sources: TPB, PMT, Cognitive Appraisal Theory, scam victimization, online fraud, revictimization, Malaysian scam studies.
3. Legal/regulatory/court material: Acts, enforcement actions, regulatory warnings, clone-firm findings.
4. News reports: Bernama, NST, The Star, etc. Use mainly for specific cases or official quotes.
5. Vendor/security blogs: use only for technical mechanism or scenario design.

## 9. Suggested Final Research Paper Direction

A strong final paper could use this structure:

1. Introduction
   - Malaysia cyber fraud problem.
   - Why revictimization matters.
   - Why awareness alone is not enough.
   - Research aim, questions, objectives, scope, contribution.

2. Literature Review
   - Cyber fraud and scam types in Malaysia.
   - Revictimization and repeat vulnerability.
   - TPB, PMT, and Cognitive Appraisal Theory.
   - Seven categories of revictimization factors.
   - Gaps in existing prevention approaches.

3. Conceptual Framework
   - Define revictimization outcome.
   - Map seven factor categories to theory constructs.
   - Show the relationship between factors, protective intention, protective behaviour, reporting behaviour, recovery behaviour, and repeat risk.

4. Methodology
   - Quantitative survey plus scenario-based assessment.
   - Target respondents: Malaysian digital users, prior victims, repeat victims, attempted-scam exposed non-victims, and no-known-exposure respondents.
   - Instrument sections: demographics, platform exposure, victimization history, seven factors, TPB/PMT/appraisal constructs, scenario tasks, tool-use capability, module feedback.
   - Sampling, ethics, pilot testing, expert validation, reliability, validity.

5. Intervention Module Design
   - Module 1: scam and revictimization awareness.
   - Module 2: scam cue detection.
   - Module 3: threat and coping appraisal.
   - Module 4: verification and reporting routine.
   - Module 5: recovery support and fake recovery scam resistance.

6. Evaluation Plan or Results
   - Pre-test/post-test.
   - Scenario safe-response score.
   - Protective self-efficacy.
   - Response efficacy.
   - Reporting intention and reporting speed.
   - Recovery-scam resistance.
   - Reduction in risky compliance.

7. Discussion
   - Which factors are expected to matter most.
   - Why Malaysia-specific scam context matters.
   - How theory explains repeated vulnerability.
   - Implications for universities, banks, government agencies, cybersecurity awareness teams, and public education.

8. Conclusion
   - Main contribution.
   - Limitations.
   - Future work.

## 10. Possible System or Public Academic Tool

If the project later needs a system, the best system idea would be:

**Scam Revictimization Risk and Training System for Malaysian Users**

Core features:

- Anonymous risk self-assessment.
- Prior victimization and repeat exposure screening.
- Seven-factor risk profile.
- Scenario-based scam simulator.
- Safe-response scoring.
- Training modules for pause, verify, refuse, report, preserve evidence, and recover safely.
- Malaysia reporting/verification guide: NSRC 997, Semak Mule, Cyber999, bank hotline, PDRM, Securities Commission tools.
- Recovery-scam warning module for prior victims.
- Researcher dashboard for anonymized pre/post improvement.

Important safety boundaries:

- Do not collect passwords, bank details, account numbers, IC numbers, or real scam evidence unless ethics approval and secure handling exist.
- Keep scenarios fictional but realistic.
- Include clear emergency guidance: if money was transferred recently, contact bank and NSRC 997 immediately.
- Treat victims respectfully and avoid victim-blaming language.

This system would fit both public benefit and academic evaluation because it can generate measurable pre/post scenario scores without requiring unsafe personal data.
